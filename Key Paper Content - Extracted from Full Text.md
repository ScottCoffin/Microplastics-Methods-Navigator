## 1. SWRCB/DDW 2025 — Collection SOP (Tier 1 Regulatory)
**"Laboratory Methods for Collection of Drinking Water Samples for Subsequent Analysis Using Raman or uFTIR Spectroscopy"**
*State Water Resources Control Board / Division of Drinking Water, March 19, 2025*

### Scope
- Describes how source and treated drinking water samples should be collected at drinking water treatment facilities (DWT)
- For subsequent analysis by Raman or FTIR microscopy
- Extraction from filters and additional processing described elsewhere (→ SWB-MP1/MP2)

### Key Method Steps
1. **Surfactant preparation**: 0.05% MP-free Tween® 20 in Type I/II ASTM water; filter through ≤0.45 µm; use within 1 week
2. **Equipment**: Two 47 mm stainless steel in-line filter holders in series (20 µm upstream, 5 µm downstream); all metal/glass construction
3. **Cleaning**: Sonicate in 0.05% surfactant solution (400 kHz, 600 W, 10 min); triple rinse
4. **Assembly**: All work in Class II BSC/laminar flow hood; turn on ≥1 hr before use; cover all openings with aluminum foil
5. **Flushing**: Flush tap ≥15 min; temperature stable ±1°C; turbidity stable ±0.5 NTU over 5 min
6. **Sample volumes**: 0.5–1 L source water; 10–20 L treated water (preliminary trials may be needed)
7. **Disassembly**: Return to BSC; triple rinse all internal components into collection beaker; this becomes the sample for subsequent processing

### Contamination Control Requirements
- Class II BSC/laminar flow hood for all assembly/disassembly
- Use glass or metal equipment wherever possible
- Purple nitrile gloves (minimal contamination potential; visually distinct)
- Cover glassware with aluminum foil (not plastic caps)
- ISO Class 5 clean room guidelines recommended where possible
- Polymer materials: record all polymers used; may be excluded from analysis

### QA/QC Requirements
- **Field blanks**: 1 per sampling event, or every 10 samples; prepared identically to samples but not connected to tap
- Record: date, replicate #, matrix, filtered volume, filter sizes (order), turbidity, line pressure, filtration time
- Training video available: https://vimeo.com/1063685718 (password: 3535)

## 3. Brander et al. 2020 — Sampling and QA/QC Guide (Tier 3)
**"Sampling and quality assurance and quality control: a guide for scientists investigating the occurrence of microplastics across matrices"**
*Applied Spectroscopy 74(9):1099–1125*

### Key Framework (Figure 2)
A two-layer decision framework:
- **Above the dashed line**: Matrix-specific and technique-specific considerations (vary by study)
- **Below the dashed line**: Universal QA/QC considerations (apply to all studies)

### Universal QA/QC Requirements (apply to ALL matrices)
1. **Adequate replication**: Multiple samples per site; compositing/averaging recommended
2. **Positive controls**: Actual or artificial samples spiked with known plastics, treated identically to samples
3. **Field blanks**: Collect at same time as samples; expose to field conditions without collecting sample
4. **Procedural/laboratory blanks**: Process through all extraction and analysis steps without sample
5. **Blank subtraction**: Apply LOD-based threshold; LOD = mean of blanks + 3 SD (min n=3 blanks; EPA recommends n=7)
6. **Limit of detection**: Report LOD for sum of all particles within specified size range
7. **Good Laboratory Practices**: 
   - Cotton lab coats (not synthetic)
   - Lint roller on clothing before lab work
   - Cover all equipment with aluminum foil or glass
   - Use glass/metal equipment wherever possible
   - Clean surfaces with 10% nitric acid or concentrated detergent (Contrad 70 or Alconox); triple rinse with MilliQ
   - Store cleaned equipment covered/sealed

### Matrix-Specific Notes (Drinking Water)
- Sample volume determined by: (1) target volume per analytical method; (2) volume before filter clogging
- Collect volume ≥3× the concentration in field and lab blanks
- Preliminary samples required to estimate size distribution and counts/L
- Smaller particles (1–10 µm) more prevalent than larger ones in DW → may allow smaller volumes
- Accurate blank concentrations AND recoveries must be reported
- No standard methods exist (note: this was 2020; CA SOPs now exist)

### Seasonal/Temporal Considerations
- Factor in seasonal precipitation shifts
- Consider influent temporal variability at treatment facilities
- Co-locate samples across matrices to link sources to receptors

## 4. Cowger et al. 2020 — Reporting Guidelines (Tier 3)
**"Reporting guidelines to increase the reproducibility and comparability of research on microplastics"**
*Applied Spectroscopy 74(9)*

### Structure
Three formats for the same guidelines:
1. **Detailed Document** (SI, available on OSF): plain-text thorough version
2. **Checklist (Table 1)**: quick reference, check off applicable items
3. **Mind Map (Figure 2)**: interactive/visual format

### 8 Main Reporting Categories
*(All sections have "Required" items that must be reported for reproducibility)*

1. **Materials**
   - Polymer type (confirmed by spectroscopy)
   - Particle size (measured, not nominal)
   - Particle shape
   - Surface characteristics
   - Source/supplier and lot number

2. **Quality Assurance / Quality Control**
   - Positive controls
   - Negative controls / procedural blanks
   - Field blanks
   - Recovery (spike experiments)
   - Limit of detection (LOD)
   - Limit of quantification (LOQ)

3. **Data Reporting**
   - Number concentration AND mass concentration
   - Size distribution (not just mean or median)
   - Polymer type distribution
   - Raw or less-processed data preferred

4. **Field Sampling**
   - Sampling equipment (materials, dimensions)
   - Volume/area/mass sampled
   - Number of replicates
   - Environmental conditions

5. **Sample Preparation**
   - Extraction/digestion method
   - Solvents/reagents used
   - Filter type and pore size
   - Procedural blank data

6. **Identification**
   - Analytical method (FTIR, Raman, Py-GC-MS, visual)
   - Instrument specifications
   - Software used (version, library)
   - Spectral match thresholds

7. **Categorization**
   - Size categories used (bins)
   - Shape categories
   - Color categories

8. **Toxicology Considerations** *(if applicable)*
   - Particle characterization methods
   - Dosimetry approach
   - Exposure concentrations (nominal + measured)

### Key Principle
*"These reporting guidelines are not meant to completely define what should be reported but are a proposal for the minimum guidelines."*

## 5. de Ruijter et al. 2020 — QA/QC Criteria for Effect Studies (Tier 3)
**"Quality Criteria for Microplastic Effect Studies in the Context of Risk Assessment: A Critical Review"**
*Environmental Science & Technology*

### Overview
- 20 QA/QC criteria in 4 categories
- Scoring: 2 = adequate; 1 = adequate with restrictions; 0 = inadequate
- 105 studies reviewed; average score: **44.6%** (range 20–77.5%)
- **No study scored positively on ALL criteria**

### The 20 QA/QC Criteria

**CATEGORY A: PARTICLE CHARACTERIZATION (Criteria 1–5)**
| # | Criterion | Minimum Requirement |
|---|-----------|---------------------|
| 1 | Particle size | Report full size distribution (≥10 bins if range used); single size with measurement error |
| 2 | Particle shape | Measured and reported; high resolution image required |
| 3 | Polymer type | Confirmed with FTIR, Raman, or similar method |
| 4 | Source of MP | Origin/production reported in detail; stock/solution source specified |
| 5 | Data reporting | Concentrations reported as **both mass AND number** |

**CATEGORY B: EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN (Criteria 6–12)**
| # | Criterion | Minimum Requirement |
|---|-----------|---------------------|
| 6 | Chemical purity | Rule out additive/surfactant toxicity; **clean MPs with organic solvent** |
| 7 | Laboratory preparation | All materials MP-free; all equipment washed with high-quality water; prevent air contamination; **use cotton lab coats** |
| 8 | Background contamination | Quantify contamination; evaluate and quantify with FTIR, Raman, or similar |
| 9 | Verification of exposure | Measure actual exposure concentration; demonstrate **≥80% of nominal** throughout test |
| 10 | Homogeneity of exposure | Verify mixing in water (photo/measurement); describe method for sediment homogeneity |
| 11 | Exposure assessment | Quantitative measurement of organism exposure (FTIR or Raman); digestion step if ingested |
| 12 | Replication | **≥3 replicates** |

**CATEGORY C: APPLICABILITY IN RISK ASSESSMENT (Criteria 13–16)**
| # | Criterion | Minimum Requirement |
|---|-----------|---------------------|
| 13 | Endpoints | Community (bacteria, algae) or individual level (survival, mortality, growth, reproduction) |
| 14 | Natural food particles | Add food to avoid force-feeding of MP; not applicable to algae/bacteria |
| 15 | Effect thresholds | Report L(E)Cx **with error/uncertainty intervals** |
| 16 | Dose-response relationship | **≥6 doses** including treatment control; full shape of effect curve |

**CATEGORY D: ECOLOGICAL RELEVANCE (Criteria 17–20)**
| # | Criterion | Minimum Requirement |
|---|-----------|---------------------|
| 17 | Concentration range | **>1 environmentally relevant concentration**; motivate concentrations with measured environmental concentrations (MECs) |
| 18 | Aging and biofouling | Use aged/biofouled particles; crucial for uptake realism |
| 19 | Diversity of MP | Test multiple polymer types; avoid monoculture of PS spheres |
| 20 | Exposure time | Longer exposures; correspond to standard ecotoxicity test guidelines where possible |

### Most Critical Gaps Found in Literature
1. Avoiding and verifying **background contamination** (most urgent)
2. Environmental relevance of **exposure conditions**
3. Studies average only 44.6% of maximum score — significant room for improvement

### Effect Mechanisms with Quality-Weighted Evidence
Only 3 mechanisms remained well-supported after quality weighting:
1. **Inhibition of food assimilation / decreased nutritional value of food**
2. **Internal physical damage**
3. **External physical damage**

## 6. Cowger et al. 2024 — Sub-sampling (Tier 4)
**"How many microplastics do you need to (sub)sample?"**
*Ecotoxicology and Environmental Safety 275:116243*

### Key Formula
**n = error⁻²** (simplified approximation)

For error = 0.05 (5%), confidence interval = 95%, unknown expected proportion, large sample (>100k particles):
- **Minimum n = 386 particles** for polymer distribution characterization
- **Minimum n = 620 particles** for polymer + color + size + morphology simultaneously

### Application
- Applies to: polymer characterization sub-sampling AND minimum count for meaningful environmental characterization
- Particles must be **randomly selected** from full population of suspected particles
- Also applies as minimum sample size for detecting particle type presence and diversity in environmental compartment

### Practical Guidance
- If <386 particles in a sample, consider this a detection limit issue, not a sub-sampling issue
- Random selection from ALL suspected particles, not cherry-picked
- The formula is a priori — use it to plan before analysis, not post-hoc

## CROSS-CUTTING SYNTHESIS: What the Papers Actually Specify

### On Blanks — Across Multiple Papers
| Blank Type | Requirement | Source |
|------------|-------------|--------|
| Field blank | 1 per sampling event or every 10 samples | SWRCB/DDW 2025 |
| Procedural/lab blank | Through all extraction and analysis steps | Brander 2020, Cowger 2020b |
| Positive control | Known MPs, treated identically to samples | Brander 2020, de Ruijter 2020 |
| Minimum n for LOD | n=3 blanks minimum; EPA recommends n=7 | Brander 2020 |
| LOD calculation | Mean of blanks + 3 SD | Brander 2020 |
| Blank correction | Must be applied; report before and after | Cowger 2020b, Dawson 2023 |

### On Particle Characterization — Minimum to Report
- Polymer type (confirmed by spectroscopy, not assumed)
- Size: full distribution (≥10 bins if range; otherwise single size with error)
- Shape (with high-resolution image)
- Source/origin of particles
- Mass AND number concentration (not just one)

### On Environmental Relevance of Exposure Materials (Toxicology)
- **Avoid**: PS spheres without justification (not representative)
- **Avoid**: unwashed particles (preservatives toxic) — Criterion 6
- **Use**: Multiple polymer types, environmentally relevant sizes
- **Use**: Aged/biofouled particles where possible
- **Motivate**: All concentrations with reference to measured environmental concentrations

### On Reporting
- Software name AND version AND spectral library source
- Spectral match thresholds used (minimum HQI/similarity >70% per Schymanski 2021 and Open Specy 1.0)
- Raw/less-processed data preferred
- Report recovery from spiked blanks

### On Spectral Analysis Tools — Consistency Requirements
| Tool | Library Size | Match Threshold | Validated For |
|------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Open Specy 1.0 | >40,000 spectra | 0.7 (0-1 scale) | FTIR + Raman, batch/hyperspectral |
| Schymanski framework | Lab-specific | HQI >70% | Single-particle micro-Raman/FTIR |
- Must report: software name, version, library used, match threshold applied
- Spectra below threshold must be labeled "unknown" and require additional evidence

### On Toxicology Data Quality (Summary Across Papers)
| Paper | Studies Reviewed | Pass Rate | Key Failure |
|-------|-----------------|-----------|-------------|
| de Ruijter 2020 | 105 ecotox | 0% all criteria; avg 44.6% | Background contamination, environmental relevance |
| Gouin 2022 (NMP-TSAT) | 74 human health | 16% Tier 1 | Dose-response insufficient for POD; PS sphere overuse |
| ToMEx 2.0 (2025) | ~300 ecotox (aquatic) | 12% threshold criteria | <3 test concentrations; no dose-response |

**Persistent finding across all years**: PS spheres dominate; dose-response data insufficient; particle characterization incomplete.

## 7. Schymanski et al. 2021 — Minimum Requirements for Spectroscopic Analysis in Clean Water (Tier 3)
**"Analysis of microplastics in drinking water and other clean water samples with micro-Raman and micro-infrared spectroscopy: minimum requirements and best practice guidelines"**
*Analytical and Bioanalytical Chemistry, 2021*
*12 European laboratories (Germany, France, Italy, Spain) — consensus paper*

### Scope
- Micro-Raman and micro-FTIR analysis for drinking water, tap water, bottled water, and clean freshwater
- Particles-based spectroscopic methods (number, size, shape, polymer type per particle)
- Focus: harmonizing methods across labs to enable inter-study comparisons

### Key Minimum Requirements (Table 1 Summary)

**Contamination Control:**
- Lab type: clean lab minimum (laminar flow best practice); linoleum/tiling floor (no carpet)
- Lab coat: cotton (not synthetic); N95 mask if needed; wash hands, tie back hair
- Gloves: check for contamination potential; purple nitrile is common choice

**Sampling Container:**
- Glass or stainless steel (avoid plastic); mechanical cleaning + rinsing minimum
- Minimum 1 replicate (best practice: minimum 3)
- Volume adapted to water type and expected particle density

**Filtration:**
- Filter pore size must match the smallest particle size reported
- Filter materials for automated systems: silicon, Au/Al-coated polycarbonate, aluminum oxide, PTFE
- Avoid plastic filter holders where alternatives exist; PTFE results should be excluded or carefully checked
- Rinse glassware once with particle-free water after filtration

**Laboratory Blanks:**
- Frequency: 1 blank per series/day, for maximum of 10 samples
- Acceptance criterion: sum of all MPs in blank must **not exceed the LOD** of the method
- Exception: if high contamination of a single polymer type, lab may exclude that polymer and report contamination details

**Analysis — Spectral Identification:**
- Hit Quality Index (HQI) threshold: minimum **>70%** for automatic acceptance
- Lab must verify auto-identification above HQI is correct via human review of characteristic peaks
- Alternative to HQI: CLS (classical least squares), RDF (random decision forest) classifiers — all require validation
- Spectral resolution: IR **≤8 cm⁻¹**; Raman: sufficient for targeted size

**Instrumentation:**
- IR acquisition range: minimum **1250–3600 cm⁻¹** (best practice: full MID-IR)
- Raman range: minimum **200–2000 cm⁻¹** (best practice: 50–4000 cm⁻¹)
- Raman laser: **532 nm or 785 nm**; laser parameters must not cause particle destruction
- ATR mode: only for particles >100 µm

**Polymer Types — Minimum Library:**
PE, PET, PP, PS, PC, PVC, PMMA, PTFE, nylon (PA), PU (several types)
- Include natural materials (proteins, cellulose) to avoid misidentification

**Size Reporting Bins (if binned):**
1–5 µm | 5–10 µm | 10–20 µm | 20–50 µm | 50–100 µm | 100–500 µm | >500 µm

**Expected particle numbers in 1 L clean water:**
- Lower size limit 5 µm → up to 10,000 particles
- Lower size limit 1 µm → >1 million particles (per Oßmann, unpublished)

### Key Principle
*"The minimum requirements and the best practice approaches are designed to (i) improve the reliability of MP analysis by helping labs to identify pitfalls in the analytical method and thus to avoid generating false-positive and false-negative results; (ii) facilitate and improve the planning of MP analysis."*

## 8. Hampton et al. 2025 — ToMEx 2.0 (Tier 3/4 — Toxicology Database)
**"The Toxicity of Microplastics Explorer (ToMEx) 2.0"**
*Microplastics and Nanoplastics, Volume 5, 2025*
*URL: https://microplastics.sccwrp.org*

### Overview
- Open-source, open-access database and web application for microplastics toxicity data
- Aquatic organisms + human health databases
- ToMEx 2.0: roughly doubled in size from 1.0; crowd-sourced international update

### Database Size (ToMEx 2.0 vs 1.0)
| Metric | ToMEx 1.0 | ToMEx 2.0 |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Aquatic studies | 162 | 286 |
| Aquatic data points | 5,871 | 12,798 |
| Aquatic species | 109 | 164 |
| Human health studies | 55 | 78 |
| Human health data points | 3,904 | 7,499 |
| Unique polymer types (aquatic) | 13 | 21 |

### Critical Finding on Data Quality
> **89% of studies in ToMEx 2.0 failed minimum screening criteria for threshold derivation**
> Only **12% of studies** pass minimum acceptability criteria (same as ToMEx 1.0 = 13%)
> Most common failure: insufficient number of test concentrations (<3) — half of aquatic studies, quarter of human health studies

### Minimum Screening Criteria (for threshold derivation)
Studies must meet pre-determined minimum quality criteria including:
1. Adequate dose–response data (≥3 test concentrations)
2. Sufficient particle characterization
3. Appropriate experimental controls
4. Organism/population-level endpoints (for thresholds 3 and 4)

### Thresholds Derived from ToMEx 2.0 (Aquatic, Marine, Food Dilution Mechanism)
| Threshold | Level | particles/L |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| #1 Investigative Monitoring | lowest concern | 5 × 10⁻⁵ |
| #2 Discharge Monitoring | | 3 × 10⁻³ |
| #3 Management Planning | | 0.3 |
| #4 Source Control | highest concern | 1 |

Note: Marine thresholds decreased dramatically (−5000 to −29-fold) from ToMEx 1.0; freshwater were more stable.

### Key Message
*"Its utility remains limited for environmental managers as 89% of studies in ToMEx 2.0 failed to meet minimum screening criteria for threshold derivation, highlighting the need to generate fit-for-purpose toxicity data for threshold development."*

## 9. Cowger et al. 2025 — Open Specy 1.0 (Tier 3 — Analytical Tool)
**"Open Specy 1.0: Automated (Hyper)spectroscopy for Microplastics"**
*Analytical Chemistry, Volume 97, 2025*
*DOI: 10.1021/acs.analchem.5c00962*

### Overview
- Open-source platform for microplastic spectral analysis (Raman + FTIR)
- Version 1.0 adds: automated batch analysis, hyperspectral image analysis, ML classifiers, expanded library
- Part of California accredited laboratory workflow

### Library Specifications
- **>40,000 open-source spectra** across 34 databases (~20,000 FTIR, ~20,000 Raman)
- Previous version: 11 databases, 5,416 spectra
- Standardized to 34 unique material descriptors (consolidated from 11,000 unstandardized material labels)

### Analytical Capabilities
- Batch analysis and hyperspectral image analysis
- Two ML classifiers: logistic regression + k medoids
- Particle identification, sizing, morphology from hyperspectral images
- Spectral smoothing, particle compression, particle splitting algorithms

### Performance Benchmarks (Accredited Standard)
- Required CV (coefficient of variation): **<40%** (accredited standard)
- Hyperspectral smoothing: **96% combined recovery rate**, CV = 38% ✓
- Without smoothing: 86% recovery, CV = 83% ✗ (fails accredited standard)
- Compression (pooled spectra per particle): >3× faster, similar accuracy, lower variability

### Match Threshold
- Spectral similarity scored 0–1; threshold **0.7** used to differentiate confident from unconfident
- Spectra below 0.7 relabeled as "unknown" — require additional lines of evidence (Visual, Raman, Py-GC-MS)
- Equivalent to HQI 70% in Schymanski 2021 framework

### Key Challenge Areas
- False positives/negatives remain significant challenge in automated ID
- Particle splitting algorithms still being refined
- Sample preparation (particle stabilization/dispersion) affects automated results

### Data Sharing
- Open Specy platform hosts open-source spectral library
- Designed to integrate with One4All reporting workflow

## 10. Gouin et al. 2022 — NMP-TSAT (Tier 3 — Human Health Tox Assessment Tool)
**"Screening and prioritization of nano- and microplastic particle toxicity studies for evaluating human health risks – development and application of a toxicity study assessment tool"**
*Microplastics and Nanoplastics, Volume 2, 2022*
*DOI: 10.1186/s43591-021-00023-x*

### Overview
- NMP Toxicity Screening Assessment Tool (NMP-TSAT) for human health effects studies
- 74 studies evaluated (inhalation and oral exposure pathways)
- 3 assessment categories; critical vs. non-critical criteria distinguished

### Scoring System
- Score per criterion: 0 = inadequate | 1 = adequate with restrictions | 2 = adequate
- Max Total Assessment Score (TAS): **52 for in vivo** | **46 for in vitro** (26 and 23 criteria respectively)
- Observed TAS range: 12–44 (in vivo); 16–34 (in vitro)
- Tiered approach: Tier 1 = all critical criteria score ≥1; Tier 2 = expert evaluation

### 3 Assessment Categories
**A. Particle Characterization:** polymer type, size, shape, surface characteristics, source/production, purity/cleaning
**B. Experimental Design:** laboratory preparation (MP-free materials), background contamination quantification, exposure verification (≥80% nominal), homogeneity, replication, controls
**C. Applicability for Risk Assessment:** dose-response (≥3 concentrations, POD derivable), species/cell model relevance, OECD-alignment where applicable

### Critical Criteria (must score ≥1 for Tier 1 prioritization)
- Polymer type confirmed
- Particle size reported
- Dose-response relationship capable of deriving a POD (NOAEL/LOAEL/BMD)
- Exposure concentration verified
- Adequate controls included

### Key Findings from 74 Studies
- **~60% used monodisperse spheres; ~46% used polystyrene**
- Most particles <5 µm; range 10 nm – 200 µm
- Only **10 oral + 2 inhalation** studies passed Tier 1 (all critical criteria scored ≥1)
- Dominant gap: lack of dose–response data sufficient for POD derivation

### Key Message
*"A need for the generation and access to standard reference materials representative of human exposure to NMPs for use in toxicity test systems and/or the improved characterization and verification of test particle characteristics, and the adoption of study design guidance, such as recommended by OECD."*

## ⚠️ KEY GAPS CONFIRMED BY PAPER CONTENT

1. **Brander 2020 (written 2020)** explicitly states: *"Currently, no standard methods exist for microplastic analyses in drinking water (but regulatory requirements are emerging)."* — This is now outdated; CA SOPs (SWB-MP1/2, SWRCB/DDW 2025) fill this gap.

2. **de Ruijter 2020** found NO study scored positively on all 20 criteria. Average = 44.6%. Field has improved since 2020 (Kennedy et al. 2025 tracks trends) but gaps remain.

3. **Cowger 2020b** explicitly states guidelines are *"a proposal for the minimum"* — not a regulatory standard.

4. **Sub-sampling threshold**: 386 particles is the minimum for polymer distribution alone. Many environmental studies report far fewer confirmed particles, calling data quality into question.

5. **ToMEx 2.0 (Hampton 2025)**: 89% of toxicology studies fail minimum screening criteria — and this has NOT improved since ToMEx 1.0 (13% pass rate in 1.0 vs. 12% in 2.0). Polystyrene spheres still dominate (>46% of studies). Lack of dose–response data is the single most cited gap for risk assessment applicability.

6. **Gouin 2022 (NMP-TSAT)**: Only 12 of 74 human health studies (10 oral + 2 inhalation) passed Tier 1 screening (all critical criteria scored ≥1). Confirms that human health toxicology studies lag far behind environmental studies in quality.

7. **Schymanski 2021**: First multi-lab consensus paper establishing minimum requirements for micro-Raman/FTIR in clean water. HQI >70% threshold now widely adopted. Note: this paper covers analysis only — collection/extraction requirements handled separately (see SWRCB/DDW 2025, SWB-MP1/2).

8. **Open Specy 1.0 (Cowger 2025)**: Automated spectral analysis now achieves accredited standard (<40% CV) only WITH hyperspectral smoothing (96% recovery); without it, CV = 83% (fails). This underlines that method details matter even within the same platform — researchers must specify the exact workflow used.

## 11. Koelmans et al. 2025 — Towards Regulatory Readiness (Tier 4 Preprint — RA Frameworks)
**"Towards Regulatory Readiness: Evaluating Frameworks for Microplastic Health Risk Assessment"**
*Preprint (SSRN/bioRxiv), 2025*
*DOI: 10.2139/ssrn.5957574*
*Authors: Koelmans, Bouwmeester, Christopher, Coffin, Haase, Lane, Legler, Vermeulen, Visser, Vogel, Wardani, Zou*

### Overview
- Comparative analysis of six published MNP human health risk assessment frameworks (RAFs)
- Proposes nine technical criteria for a scientifically sound framework
- Assigns Technology Readiness Level (TRL) and regulatory readiness ratings to each framework
- Recommends synthesis of best elements from multiple frameworks into a harmonized approach

### Nine Criteria for a Scientifically Sound MNP Risk Assessment Framework (Table 1)
1. **Substance identification & characterization** — reflects the plastic continuum (size, shape, polymer, aging state) as continuous distributions
2. **Quantitative external exposure assessment** — food, air, water; probabilistic; standardized methods
3. **Internal biodistribution (PBK)** — links external intake to organ-level concentrations; QIVIVE for in vitro–in vivo extrapolation
4. **Quantitative hazard assessment** — dose-response curves; BMD/BMDL derivation; AOPs/MIEs; environmentally realistic test particles
5. **Risk characterization** — integrates exposure and hazard; harmonized metrics; transparent uncertainty
6. **Addresses plastic-associated chemicals** — residual monomers, additives, environmental contaminants; mixture principles (EFSA, WHO)
7. **QA/QC limitations framework** — data quality scoring; study selection criteria; transparency
8. **Regulatory alignment** — outputs expressed in regulatory-actionable form (risk quotients, guidance values, reference doses)
9. **Technology/regulatory readiness** — demonstrated operational use; compatible with EFSA, WHO, REACH, US EPA expectations

### Technology Readiness Level (TRL) Ratings (Table 2)
| Framework | TRL | Key Reason |
|-----------|-----|-----------|
| SCCWRP (Coffin et al. 2022) | **7** | Adopted by CA SWRCB; operational in regulatory drinking water context |
| MICROPLASTICLAB (Koelmans 2017–2025) | **6** | Full probabilistic framework; peer-reviewed; not yet formally adopted in regulation |
| POLYRISK (Vogel 2024) | **5** | Validated prototype; real-world exposure scenarios; not yet operationally tested |
| MOMENTUM (2025) | **5** | Case-specific MOE-based indoor air inhalation; partial quantification |
| AURORA (Christopher 2024) | **3–4** | Largely conceptual; early-life/maternal focus; key modules forthcoming |
| PLASTICHEAL / PlasticRiskCat (Hansen 2023) | **3** | Qualitative hazard scoring only; proof-of-concept |

### Regulatory Readiness Ratings (Table 3)
| Framework | Regulatory Readiness | Assessment |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------|
| SCCWRP | **Highest** | Already implemented in CA; uses OEHHA methodologies; highly pragmatic |
| MICROPLASTICLAB | **High** | Aligned with EFSA/REACH/WHO; ecological RA implemented by SCCWRP & RIVM; not officially adopted for human health |
| POLYRISK | **Moderate** | Closely aligned with WHO/ECHA; scenario-based; not yet applied regulatorily |
| AURORA | **Low–Moderate** | Designed for REACH/ECHA; structured but largely conceptual |

### Key Messages & Actionable Items for Quality Standards Project
1. **QA/QC tools are a defining feature of the most advanced frameworks** — MICROPLASTICLAB uses systematic quality screening + AI tools (Qiu et al. 2025) as gateway to all quantitative steps. This supports the manuscript argument that QA/QC standards are prerequisite to RA.
2. **SCCWRP at TRL7** confirms California's regulatory framework is the most implementation-ready globally — directly relevant to Scott's regulatory context.
3. **Harmonized metrics (TRMs) are the linchpin** — without consistent units between exposure and effect data, risk characterization is unreliable. Our crosswalk should flag whether guidance papers address metric harmonization.

5. **"No framework is yet fully ready"** — but SCCWRP is closest for regulatory use; MICROPLASTICLAB is most complete scientifically. Synthesis of both is the recommended path forward.

## 12. De Ruijter et al. 2025 — Brief History of MP Effect Testing (Tier 3 — Ecotoxicology Review)
**"A brief history of microplastics effect testing: Guidance and prospect"**
*Environmental Pollution, Volume 368, 2025*
*DOI: 10.1016/j.envpol.2025.125711*
*Authors: De Ruijter, Redondo-Hasselerharm, Koelmans*

### Overview
- Comprehensive review of MP ecotoxicology testing from 2016 to 2024
- Summarizes QA/QC evolution, available guidelines, risk assessment approaches
- Provides step-by-step protocol for creating polydisperse environmentally relevant MP (ERMP) test materials
- Higher-tier community-level analysis finds: **no risks expected for freshwater benthic communities at current environmental concentrations**

### Core Finding: Same Issues from 2016 Persist in 2024
> *"Numerous reviews have consistently highlighted the shortcomings of studies evaluating the effects of microplastics (MP), with many of the issues identified in 2016 still relevant in 2024."*

From **de Ruijter et al. 2020** analysis (n=105 studies):
- No study scored positively on ALL 20 QA/QC criteria
- Most room for improvement: experimental design (contamination control, exposure verification, concentration homogeneity)
- For RA applicability: need effect thresholds + ≥6 dose levels
- For ecological relevance: aged/biofouled particles preferred over pristine

### Key QA/QC Failures Still Common in 2024
1. Inadequate particle characterization (polymer type, size, shape)
2. Insufficient contamination prevention and measurement
3. No exposure concentration verification (target: ≥80% of nominal)
4. Single or too-few test concentrations (cannot derive POD/dose-response)
5. Pristine monodisperse polystyrene spheres tested instead of environmentally relevant particles
6. Fibres underrepresented in laboratory tests (despite dominance in field samples)
7. Chemical-associated MP tests using unrealistically high plastic-to-water ratios (up to 80 g/L)
8. Parallel exposure routes (food, water) not considered when testing plastic-associated chemicals

### Polydisperse ERMP Protocol (Key Actionable Item)
The paper provides a step-by-step protocol (Supporting Information) for creating **Environmentally Relevant Microplastic (ERMP)** test mixtures:
- Must reflect realistic polymer composition, particle size distribution, and shape categories (fibres, fragments, pellets, sheets)
- Must meet de Ruijter et al. (2020) QA/QC criteria
- Enables direct risk characterization without computational alignment corrections
- Implemented across 16 invertebrate species in de Ruijter et al. 2023

### Data Rescaling & Alignment Framework
- **Problem:** MPs in lab studies differ from MPs in environment — different size, shape, polymer, concentration units
- **Solution (Koelmans et al. 2020):** Rescale exposure and effect data to a common metric using probability density functions (PDFs)
- **Preference:** Use ERMP directly rather than computing corrections (less uncertainty)
- Key metric options: volume-based (food dilution mechanism) or surface area-based (translocation mechanism)

### Natural Particle Controls
- Natural mineral particles (as similar as possible in dimensions to MP) should be used as controls
- Provides insight into food dilution mechanism (volume is the critical determinant)
- De Ruijter et al. 2025: tested ERMP vs near-identical non-polymer particle mixture

### Freshwater Ecological Risk Assessment Result (Higher-Tier)
Using community-level NOEC data (Naididae, Redondo-Hasselerharm et al. 2020) + data rescaling:
- Rescaled PNEC (volume, AF=0): 2.68 × 10¹⁰ particles/kg
- Rescaled PNEC (volume, AF=5): 5.5 × 10⁹ particles/kg
- **Conclusion: No freshwater benthic community risks expected at current environmental concentrations**
- Note: This is higher-tier and not directly applicable to regulatory action thresholds
