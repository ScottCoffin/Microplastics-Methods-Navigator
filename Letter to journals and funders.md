1. **Background  \- Todd** (ideally I have aimed to keep this brief and concise, the goal of which is perhaps 3-4 paragraphs, simply set the scene, below a preliminary draft, to be expanded upon in the following two sections with a bit more detail)

Microplastics work is spanning many fields and subfields- with some having a decade of research including trial and error into methods and practices, with others new to the work. To have high reliability data across fields, funded projects and publications should meet a minimum set of criteria in their work.

Concerns about the implications that exposure to micro- and nanoplastic particles (MNPs) represent to human health have been highlighted as a key focus area included in the recently published Lancet Countdown on health and plastics (10.1016/s0140-6736(25)01447-3). Specifically, Landrigan et al. (2025) raise awareness of studies reporting MNPs in various environmental media as well as in human tissues, but note the need for further validation of data reporting MNPs \<10 𝜇m.  The analytical challenges of reliably detecting MNPs \<10 𝜇m have been well documented (refs), with uncertainties arising with both spectroscopic and spectrometric methods.  Reliable data reporting human exposure to MNPs \<10 𝜇m are of particular importance, as it is understood that particles \<10 𝜇m have a higher probability to translocate across cellular membranes, and therefore have the potential to cause an adverse effect on cells and tissues that they may interact with, within an individual.   Characterizing and quantifying the properties and concentrations of MNPs \<10 𝜇m thus represents an important data need, whereby an improved understanding of exposure can help to better inform the design of toxicity studies, which can thus be based on the use of a suite of MNPs with environmentally relevant properties and concentrations.    
Given the ambitions of the Lancet Countdown, the goal of which is to support the generation of a credible monitoring system to track progress towards the reduction of plastic exposures, such as to MNPs, it is thus prudent that data generated to support the activity seriously consider the adoption of best practices in the analysis and reporting of MNPs. Our goal is to support the initiatives of the Lancet Countdown through this communication, which **aims to emphasize the importance of adopting robust quality assurance and quality control practices in both the analysis of monitoring data and toxicity effects testing.**  Over the last several years, multiple studies have been published recommending best practices in microplastics research (refs) and critical QA/QC criteria (refs), in the context of both environmental monitoring and toxicity testing.  An important and consistent recommendation from these studies is the need for a suite of model reference MNPs that can be used to support analytical method development and toxicity testing (refs).  In the absence of model reference particles we note that many studies continue to rely on the use of polystyrene spheres and/or the use of poorly characterized MNPs generated using a variety of non-standard methods (10.1186/s12989-024-00599-1; 10.1186/s12989-024-00581-x).

4. **Working within our realities** \- (Susanne) \-1000 words  
     
   In an ideal world with sufficient access to resources and adequate funding, as well as having standardized protocols and particles, it would be possible for all labs to meet the quality assurance and control expectations described above. However, despite over twenty years of relatively rapid progress in the micro and now nanoplastics research field, methodological advances as well as data generation (e.g. sample size limitations) remain resource-constrained for many laboratories. This is in part due to funding constraints, which are a persistent issue for the environmental sciences writ large, but also due to the nature of protocols necessary to ensure accurate characterization and quantification of particles. Extraction of microplastics from complex matrices often require many hours per sample (there is a paper that estimated this to be on the order of 40-60?) or alternatively, the creation of high volumes of particles from synthetic materials for use in laboratory exposures necessitates specific expertise and instrumentation…
