## 46 

## **Abstract:** 

- 47 The ubiquitous pollution of the environment with microplastics - a diverse suite of 

- 48 contaminants - is of growing concern for science and currently receives considerable 

- 49 public, political, and academic attention. The potential impact of microplastics in the 

- 50 environment has prompted a great deal of research in recent years. Many diverse 

- 51 methods have been developed to answer different questions about microplastic 

- 52 pollution, from sources, transport, and fate in the environment, and about effects on 

- 53 humans and wildlife. These methods are often insufficiently described, making studies 

- 54 neither comparable nor reproducible. The proliferation of new microplastic investigations 

- 55 and cross-study syntheses to answer larger scale questions are hampered. We - a 

- 56 diverse group of 23 researchers - think these issues can begin to be overcome through 

- 57 the adoption of a set of reporting guidelines. This collaboration was created using an 

- 58 open science framework that we detail for future use. Here, we suggest harmonized 

- 59 reporting guidelines for microplastic studies in environmental and laboratory settings 

- 60 through all steps of a typical study, including best practices for reporting materials, 

- 61 quality assurance/quality control, data, field sampling, sample preparation, microplastic 

- 62 identification, microplastic categorization, microplastic quantification, and considerations 

- 63 for toxicology studies. We developed three easy to use documents - a detailed 

- 64 document, a checklist, and a mind map - that can be used to reference the reporting 

- 65 guidelines quickly. We intend that these reporting guidelines support the annotation, 

- 66 dissemination, interpretation, reviewing, and synthesis of microplastic research. 

- 67 Through open access licensing (CC BY 4.0), these documents aim to increase the 

- 68 validity, reproducibility, and comparability of studies in this field for the benefit of the 

- 69 global community.

## 73 **Introduction:** 

- 74 The state of method reporting for investigations on microplastic pollution is currently at a 

- 75 turning point.[1] As this new research field evolves, it is striving to establish a harmonized 

- 76 community approach to developing, applying, and reporting methodologies. Two of the 

- 77 main purposes for reporting scientific methods are to allow for their replication and 

- 78 enable data to be directly comparable among studies. For example, in the 

- 79 environmental sciences, data from studies might be compared during risk assessments, 

- 80 synthesized for meta-analyses, or used to inform policy creation and monitoring 

- 81 guidelines. Issues with reproducibility and comparability of both data and methods are 

- 82 common across all scientific fields,[2–4] including microplastic research.[1,5,6] Here, we - a 

- 83 diverse group of 23 microplastic researchers from around the world - present a 

- 84 proposed step towards addressing this issue for microplastics, first by capturing what is 

- 85 already in published literature, and then by prioritizing which types of information should 

- 86 be included in research to reach this goal. Our four aims are to 1) review key 

- 87 reproducibility and comparability problems and solutions for microplastic research; 2) 

- 88 discuss the open science framework used to identify and prioritize key methodological 

- 89 parameters suggested here; 3) develop reporting guidelines for researchers to use 

- 90 when reporting, comparing, and developing methods; and 4) present our vision for 

- 91 future microplastic research.

## 93 **The reproducibility and comparability** 

## 94 **turning point in microplastics research** 

- 95 It is well-known that microplastics have a ubiquitous presence in the environment,[7–10] 

- 96 and the potential harm microplastics can cause to species across trophic levels has 

- 97 been recently reviewed.[11,12] While there is mixed evidence for effects, a range of 

- 98 suborganismal, organismal and population-level responses have been reported.[6,11,13] 

- 99 These results have spurred substantial research activity, as evidenced by the continued 

- 100 exponential growth in the published literature on the topic of microplastics (Figure 1). 

101 

102

- 104 and querying the field of Environmental Sciences. Publications are annual sums. The figure was 105 created using Python 3.6.9. 

- 106 

- 107 The rapid expansion of research activities and the resulting data generated in the field 

- 108 of microplastics has resulted in a diverse suite of methods and non-standardized 

- 109 approaches to reporting sample collection, extraction, and analysis.[1,14–21] Each method 

- 110 has its strengths and weaknesses, and there are continued efforts to optimize existing 

- 111 methods and develop new ones that may improve throughput, detection limit, and 

- 112 reproducibility. The development of new methods continues because currently there is 

- 113 no 'catch-all' combination of methods for sampling, extracting, analyzing, and reporting 114 microplastics that is capable of accurately characterizing and quantifying all 

- 115 microplastics present in a sample.[22,23] This is because microplastics are a diverse suite 116 of contaminants that vary greatly in morphology, chemical properties, texture, color, 

- 117 density, and size.[24] Moreover, environments and research goals are diverse and a 

- 118 universal solution is unable to capture this diversity, especially as research matures in 

- 119 this rapidly expanding field. With this in mind, methods should be chosen based on the 120 scientific question and reported with enough detail to be comparable and reproducible. 

- 121 Comparability between studies facilitates meta-analysis,[25,26] which has been difficult for 122 microplastics due to the diversity of methods employed and study details reported.[17–21] 123 Incomparability is caused by studies published without documenting the elements 124 essential for translating units and metrics to others that are commonly used in the field. 

- 125 For example, studies that employ Raman spectroscopy might not be comparable to 126 those that employ Fourier-transformed infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy if neither describes 127 their analysis and data transformation steps.[18,27] Additionally, aquatic studies that use 128 water volume grab sampling are not comparable to studies that use net sampling if the 129 studies do not describe the mesh size used, depth of sample collection, or the sample 130 volume.[28] In another example, ingestion studies on the same species of animal are not 131 comparable if they fail to mention which part of the gastrointestinal tract was analyzed 132 (e.g., just the gizzard or the gizzard and proventriculus of birds).[15,17] Moreover, a study 133 using different chemical digestion methods to measure ingestion may be incomparable 134 because some digestion procedures destroy certain plastics.[29] Regardless of diverse 135 methods and wherever possible, reporting raw - or less processed - data would allow 

- 136 reverse engineering and harmonization of some techniques. Still, raw data are seldom 137 reported.[16,30] 

- 138 Factors that cause incomparability can also hinder the reproducibility of research. 

- 139 Irreproducible research occurs, in part, when the elements that are critical for 

- 140 reproducing similar results are not elucidated. Reproducibility allows responsible 

- 141 decision-making and expansion of protocols. For example, software names should be 

- 142 reported when used because software often has proprietary algorithms and may not be 

- 143 reproducible unless the same software is used. In another example, if a study that 

- 144 employs organic matter digestion does not describe the chemical solution used, its 

- 145 manufacturer, and concentration used to digest the sample, the study cannot be 146 reproduced. 

- 147 Reporting guidelines provide a structured framework where method information critical 

- 148 to comparing and reproducing research can be referenced. There is a critical need for 

- 149 reporting guidelines in microplastic research as already initiated with the Minimum 

- 150 Information for Microplastic Studies "MIMS" concept for the study of microplastics in 

- 151 seafood,[1] the minimum information for publication of infrared-related data,[27] and other 

- 152 works assessing data quality in microplastic studies.[31–33] The reporting guidelines we 

- 153 developed attempt to build on previous work and expand the scope to more 

- 154 methodological components in microplastic research. This study leverages the expertise 

- 155 of a diverse group of researchers from around the world to cover the breadth of the 

- 156 field. To be as transparent as possible, we elaborate on the reasons why each reporting 

- 157 guideline is necessary, and provide examples for each. Other fields, like molecular 

- 158 biology,[34] proteomics,[35] and transcriptomics,[36] already have highly successful examples 

- 159 of reporting guidelines that have been widely adopted by their field, and we hope this 

- 160 work serves a similar purpose in our field.

## **Methods:** 

- 162 As a scientific community, we recognize that the need for reporting guidelines for 

- 163 microplastic methods is best addressed through a collaborative open science 

- 164 framework. With this goal in mind, the lead author sent out the following request on 

- 165 Twitter, and tagged several scientists in the microplastic community with a link to a 

- 166 collaborative document: 

- 167 

- 168 " _Frustrated with the reproducibility crisis in #microplastics research from poor method_ 

- 169 _descriptions? Now is your chance to change that. I will publish this collaborative_ 

- 170 _document OA [Open Access]. Add method considerations to this document and cite_ 

- 171 _yourself in the Ack [Acknowledgements]_ ." (Win Cowger, @Win_OpenData, June 13th, 172 2019). 

- 173 

- 174 The collaborative document was hosted open access on Google Drive and researchers 175 were invited to provide input on the reporting guidelines for microplastic research 

- 176 methods. Over the subsequent week, 15 contributors edited the shared document 

- 177 directly. After one week, all initial contributors were invited to be coauthors, and 

- 178 additional coauthors were invited by word of mouth throughout the process using an 

- 179 open door policy. Overall, there were 23 authors on this project and 26 other people 

- 180 acknowledged for their assistance. In a meeting of coauthors, the threshold for co181 authorship was set at one full day of effort (self defined and self reported), while the 182 threshold for acknowledgement was to review the document at least once. Authors 183 contributed to this publication and the reporting guideline documents. The first author 184 (Cowger) led the collaboration and the author order after the first author was 185 randomized by agreement of all coauthors. 

- 186 

- 187 The reporting guidelines were identified by referencing standard operating procedures 188 used by various authors and other peer-reviewed publications. All authors agreed not to 189 use language that would imply an intent to standardize methodology or recommend 190 specific methods over others; this was beyond the scope of the work. The task of the 191 authors in developing the reporting guidelines was to outline what should be reported 192 about a method when the method was used to make the method reproducible and 193 comparable. To determine which guidelines were essential to add to the documents, 194 each author was asked to fill out a Google Form survey where they designated each 195 reporting category as required or not. The final reporting guidelines were formed by 196 keeping only the guidelines that 51% or more of the authors agreed upon. During the 197 review process, we received requests by reviewers to add additional reporting 198 requirements. Where they were not already accounted for, we added them to the 199 reporting guidelines and indicated those additions using an asterisk throughout the 200 produced documents. The final reporting guidelines were packaged into three 201 documents which have the same information summarized with specific user groups in 

- 202 mind: 1) thorough - a Detailed Document, 2) quick and simple - a Checklist (Table 1), 

- 203 and 3) interactive -an online Mind Map (Figure 2). 

- 204 

- 205 The reporting guidelines were sent out to other colleagues in the field for an 

- 206 endorsement and critique designated as signatories in the acknowledgments. After the 

- 207 first week, we received 19 endorsements. The manuscript and supporting information 

- 208 were also subject to internal review at the National Institute of Standards and 

- 209 Technology and single blind peer review from Applied Spectroscopy. In these ways, we 

- 210 attempted to receive as much feedback as we could to develop reporting guidelines that 211 reflect the diverse group of experts and the broad scope of methods in microplastic 

- 212 research. This framework represents an example of a way that scientists in any field 

- 213 can develop robust collaborations by sharing ideas and learning from one another while 

- 214 developing useful reference documents, even if they have not met before. 

## 215 **Reporting Guideline Document** 

## 216 **Descriptions:** 

- 217 The three documents we created of the reporting guidelines include a 1) Detailed 

- 218 Document, 2) Checklist (Table 1), and 3) online Mind Map (Figure 2). Each document 219 has the same information summarized with different users in mind. These documents 

- 220 are expected to be useful for scientists researching microplastics, peer reviewers asked 

- 221 to evaluate research, and users of the data. These documents outline what needs to be 

- 222 reported for common methods in microplastic research to be reproducible and 

- 223 comparable. The documents can also be used when developing methods internally to 

- 224 quickly identify the essential components of a method to calibrate and control in a lab. 

- 225 The Detailed Document can be used when every detail listed in the reporting guidelines 

- 226 are important to know. The Checklist can be used to quickly reference the reporting 

- 227 guidelines and check off the guidelines relevant to a specific study. The Mind Map is 

- 228 useful for those who prefer interactive information workflows and want to be able to 229 quickly summarize and expand the reporting guidelines at any level of detail. 

- 230 Any of these documents can be used to reference the report guidelines. All of the 

- 231 documents contain the same information reformatted and summarized. In the 

- 232 documents, the general method groups we define are: Materials, Quality Assurance / 

- 233 Quality Control (QA/QC), Data Reporting, Field Sampling, Sample Preparation, 

- 234 Identification, Categorization, and Toxicology Considerations. Subgroups describe 

- 235 specific method techniques within each group. Some of the groups may be used more 

- 236 than once in a study while some may not be used at all. It is important to note that these 

- 237 documents are templates and one need only consider the guidelines from the groups of 

- 238 methods relevant to a given study. When using the documents, first, assess which 

- 239 groups of methods apply to the study. Subgroups of methods are tab separated to 

- 240 indicate more detailed levels of grouping. Next, assess which of the subgroups apply. 

- 241 These can be highlighted or opened for easy reference. Where the most detailed 

- 242 subgroups apply, all italicized reporting guidelines must be defined, described, or 

- 243 discussed for that method to be reproducible and comparable. All reporting guidelines 

- 244 always apply to groups that do not have subgroups. Importantly, these reporting 

- 245 guidelines are not meant to completely define what should be reported but are a 

- 246 proposal for the minimum guidelines. Below we detail each document individually and 

- 247 outline a path forward for the documents to be updated. 

## 248 Detailed Document: 

- 249 The Detailed Document (SI1; OSF) is the plain-text thorough version of the reporting 

- 250 guidelines containing the identical information, groups, and order to the Checklist and 

- 251 Mind Map described below. While this document is the primary result of this project, its 

- 252 length precludes including it in the main manuscript. The Detailed Document is meant 

- 253 for those who are new to the methods or want a detailed description and reference 

- 254 examples of the reporting guideline. This document may also be useful to those who 

- 255 find the Mind Map format to be challenging to navigate. The Detailed Document is easily 

- 256 printed for reference, which can be especially useful during the design stage of a study. 

- 257 The format of this document follows that the highest level of method grouping is in the 

- 258 largest text font and bolded. Subgroups of methods are in bold and identical font size 

- 259 but further indented if they are a subgroup of a subgroup. The essential elements to 

- 260 report are italicized and all the same font size. The explanation, reason, and examples 

- 261 for each essential element immediately follow the element and are light gray in color. 

- 262 The Checklist: 

- 263 The Checklist (SI2; OSF; Table 1) is meant for those already familiar with the methods 

- 264 and reasons for reporting outlined in the other documents. The format follows the 

- 265 Detailed Document but the explanation, reason, and examples for each reporting 

- 266 guideline are removed for quick reference and reading so that the elements can be 

- 267 checked off when reviewing or writing documents. Citations used in the Detailed 

- 268 document are added at the end of each guideline. The reporting guidelines are italicized 269 and all the same font size as in the Detailed Document. 

- 270 Table 1: This is the Checklist of the reporting guidelines. Asterisk (*) indicates that the guideline 

- 271 was added as part of peer review; all other guidelines were voted on by a majority of the 

- 272 coauthors. The guidelines are grouped using bolded and indented labels. The guidelines are 

- 273 italicized and are the furthest indented for each group. Citations correspond to additional 

- 274 information related to the guideline and good examples of reporting.

## 275 Mind Map: 

- 276 The Mind Map (SI3; LINK; OSF; Figure 2) was developed because we recognized a 

- 277 need to have many intermediate levels of detail between the detail provided by the 

- 278 Detailed Document and the Checklist. Interactive mind map documents allow the user 279 to query to the level of detail they need quickly. This is meant for users who prefer 

- 280 spatially structured interactive information queries. The Mind Map was formatted using 281 www.mindmeister.com, a free collaborative mind map creator that can reformat mind 

- 282 maps into tiered documents. The Mind Map is structured the same as the Detailed 

- 283 Document, where general method groups flow from the primary term 'Microplastics 

- 284 Reporting Guidelines.' These general groups are further refined by subgroups of 

- 285 method types and instrument groups, where the terminal node of every branch leads to 286 essential methodology elements (italicized) that should be reported. Each reporting 

- 287 guideline is described by an explanation, reasons to report, and/or examples from 

- 288 published microplastic literature.

## 295 Strategy for Updating the Reporting Guidelines: 

- 296 The field of microplastic research is rapidly evolving, and we expect that our documents, 

- 297 like most things in science, will need to be adapted, expanded, and revised. We 

- 298 recognize that as the field of microplastic pollution develops and grows, there will be 

- 299 new techniques and methods developed that will have reporting guidelines. We also 

- 300 acknowledge other methods are already useful to report that are not yet covered here. 

- 301 These documents are expected to be updated over time as new techniques are 

- 302 developed. That is why all documents are completely free and hold open access 

- 303 licenses (CC BY 4.0). The license allows for redistribution and adaptation with 

- 304 attribution to the original document. Additionally, we created an Open Science 

- 305 Framework project (OSF) for each document where researchers can reach out with 

- 306 suggestions and comments to update future editions of these documents. The authors 

- 307 will monitor the comments on the project and respond as necessary. Future versions 

- 308 will be updated periodically on the OSF project site using version control. Additionally, 

- 309 we submitted this reporting guideline and others reported in the literature[1,27] to the 

- 310 reporting guideline portal at https://fairsharing.org/. We hope that these documents and 

- 311 online forums are widely used for the benefit of the global community. 

## 312 **Our vision of the future of research on** 

## 313 **microplastics:** 

- 314 We envision a future where research on microplastics is comparable, reproducible, and 315 transparent. We aim for researchers in the field to be able to read a paper and use the 

- 316 methods for their work and/or use the data in a synthesis paper or meta-analysis. We 

- 317 aim for policy-makers and managers to be able to review the literature and have the 

- 318 ability to compare data across sources, pathways, and geographies to inform the 

- 319 decision-making process. We envision a field where communication is clear amongst 

- 320 different stakeholders in the world of microplastics and where collaboration and 

- 321 research translation are made simpler. With our collaborative and open access 

- 322 framework, we aim to improve future work on microplastics and provide a framework for 

- 323 other emerging contaminants.
