## 32 **Abstract** 

- 33 Micro- and nanoplastic particles (MNPs) have emerged as pollutants of high public concern. 34 Assessing and managing the risks of these particles remains challenging for several reasons. A 35 long-term goal is to establish a comprehensive risk-governance framework that includes risk 36 framing, scientifically sound risk assessment that accounts for human and environmental safety, 37 evaluation, and risk management/decision making. A realistic short-term goal is to develop a 38 comprehensive human health risk assessment framework (RAF). Very recently, RAFs for 39 evaluating the human-health risks of MNPs have become available; however, comparative 40 analyses of these frameworks are lacking. Here, we discuss six established frameworks to assess 41 their technological and regulatory readiness. We begin by proposing nine technical criteria that 42 a risk-assessment framework should meet to inform policy and action. These include the degree 43 of quantifiability of outputs; the provision for systematic evaluation of the quality of input data; 44 the consistency between exposure and effect data with respect to underlying mechanisms; the 45 extent to which the complexity and diversity of environmentally realistic microplastics are 46 addressed; readiness for integration into existing regulatory approaches; and the extent of real47 world implementation to date. We discuss the specific strengths of each framework and 48 recommend combining them into a single overarching framework that integrates these 49 strengths.

## 55 **Introduction** 

- 56 Microplastics are routinely detected in food, water, air, and consumer products, meaning 

- 57 exposure is widespread across the general public. Growing evidence suggests they can 

- 58 accumulate in the human body and may contribute to inflammation and oxidative stress. 

- 59 These potential impacts are of particular concern for vulnerable groups, including children 

- 60 and pregnant women.[1,2,3] 

- 61 Environmental risks of microplastics are already well recognized and have led to restrictions 

- 62 on primary microplastics under the EU chemicals regulation REACH.[4] In contrast, human- 

- 63 health risks have only recently garnered attention. A comprehensive one health risk- 

- 64 governance framework, encompassing both environmental and human health is best suited to 

- 65 address the complexity of understanding and assessing microplastics risks. For nanomaterials, 

- 66 risk governance frameworks have been developed that typically consider (i) risk framing 

- 67 (based on early warnings), (ii) risk assessment, (iii) evaluation, and (iv) risk 

- 68 management/decision-making.[5] It is advisable to involve different actors and stakeholders 

- 69 from the outset to gather all available information, capture diverse perspectives, and jointly 

- 70 develop suitable approaches. Furthermore, both the risks and benefits of plastic use should be 

- 71 considered. Despite the current focus on threats and risks, plastic materials also offer valuable 

- 72 properties and applications, which explains their widespread global use. 

- 73 Developing robust risk governance for (micro)plastics is crucial for policymakers, the public 74 and private sectors, and society alike: it provides a scientific foundation to understand the 75 potential risks of these emerging pollutants and to evaluate options for action from multiple 76 perspectives. This enables policymakers to identify priority release scenarios and exposure 77 routes, develop safety thresholds, and support regulations aimed at eliminating or reducing 78 harmful exposure when warranted (e.g., occupational exposure limits). Risk governance also 

- 79 allows targeted measures to be derived from solid science rather than precaution alone. For 

- 80 society, it offers transparency and reassurance, helping to bridge public concerns with 

- 81 credible health-protection measures. The private sector benefits through regulatory 

- 82 predictability, improved product safety, and enhanced consumer trust. As microplastic 

- 83 pollution continues to grow, risk assessment is becoming an increasingly indispensable tool 

- 84 for responsible environmental management and public health policy. A core element of risk 85 governance is a robust risk assessment framework (RAF) underpinned by sound science and a 86 transparent weight-of-evidence approach. 

- 87 The Lancet Countdown report on plastic and health has documented significant impacts of 

- 88 plastic on human health across the lifecycle of plastic.[6] In concordance with earlier reports of 89 the World Health Organization (WHO) the potential human health risks associated with 

- 90 exposure to micro- and nanoplastics (MNPs) via drinking water, food, and air remain 

- 91 uncertain.[7,8] In its 2019 assessment, the WHO concluded that at current exposure levels, there 92 was “no evidence to indicate a human health concern”, but emphasized that this does not 

- 93 equate to evidence of no risk.[7,9] The WHO reports called for further research and the 

- 94 development of robust, science-based frameworks for risk assessment of MNPs. They 

- 95 identified major knowledge gaps, including the absence of standardized methods and Quality 

- 96 Assurance/Quality Control (QA/QC) protocols, the lack of realistic test materials, and the 

- 97 need for models of internal exposure (such as physiologically based kinetic or ‘PBK’ models). 98 WHO recommended precautionary measures to reduce pollution and prioritized research into 99 exposure, toxicology, and biokinetics to enable future risk characterization based on reliable, 

- 100 harmonized, and environmentally relevant data. The question is whether, given the 

- 101 widespread presence of MNPs, using a strict definition of the precautionary principle as the 102 default strategy is sustainable. Because MNPs are everywhere, trying to avoid all potential 103 sources is neither practical nor effective in the long term. A more workable path is to adopt a 

- 104 risk-based approach grounded in quantitative data, health-relevant thresholds, and targeted 

- 105 mitigation measures that address exposure pathways of concern where acceptable health-risk 106 thresholds are exceeded. 

- 107 Partly in parallel with, and partly following, the WHO’s efforts and similar initiatives by 

- 108 SAPEA[10] , several groups and projects have attempted to develop frameworks for assessing 109 the health risks of microplastics. Examples include the California Microplastics Health 

- 110 Effects Workshop hosted by the Southern California Coastal Water Research Project 

- 111 (hereafter referred to as ‘SCCWRP’), the Wageningen MICROPLASTICLAB project cluster 

- 112 (hereafter referred to as ‘MICROPLASTICLAB’), several projects from the recent European 113 CUSP cluster (www.cusp-research.eu), such as AURORA, POLYRISK, and 

- 114 PLASTICHEAL, and the MOMENTUM project, which uses a risk assessment framework 

- 115 demonstrated through a case study (hereafter referred to as ‘MOMENTUM’). Besides the 116 AURORA, POLYRISK and PLASTICHEAL frameworks, CUSP reports two other 

- 117 frameworks, IMPTOX and PlasticsFate, which focus on allergy and asthma-related health 

- 118 outcomes and on source-fate-product-oriented risk evaluation of plastics, respectively, but 119 have not yet been published as standalone frameworks in the peer-reviewed literature.[11] 

- 120 Most of these frameworks are based on the classical risk assessment paradigm, consisting of 121 problem formulation, hazard identification, hazard characterization, exposure assessment, and 

- 122 risk characterization.[12] However, the frameworks differ in purpose, approach, completeness, 

- 123 and technological and/or regulatory maturity. Typically, best practices only become apparent 124 after project completion, so efficient cross-pollination is not guaranteed. Following similar 

- 125 evaluations of RAFs for other chemicals and nanomaterials[13,14,15] , and as more frameworks 126 for MNP are now being published, it is timely to analyse their similarities and differences and 127 assess their added value. It is also important to identify what the different approaches can 128 learn from one another, with the aim of forming an integrated body of best practices.
