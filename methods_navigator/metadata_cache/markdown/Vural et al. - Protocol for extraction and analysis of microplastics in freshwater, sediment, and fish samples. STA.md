## SUMMARY 

Microplastic pollution threatens aquatic ecosystems and food webs. Here, we present a protocol for extracting microplastics from freshwater, sediment, and fish digestive system samples. We collect water using manta nets, sample sediment with benthic grabs, and dissect fish to isolate digestive organs. We perform chemical digestion, density separation, vacuum filtration, and microscopic enumeration, followed by polymer identification using μ-Raman spectroscopy. Quality control steps ensure high recovery and minimize contamination. For complete details on the use and execution of this protocol, please refer to Gu¨ndogdu� et al.[1,2] 

## BEFORE YOU BEGIN 

This protocol includes detailed procedures for investigating the presence and diversity of microplastics (MPs) in freshwater samples collected from natural lakes using manta nets, benthic sediment samples obtained via Van Veen Grab, and fish digestive system samples collected using gillnets. The protocol is adaptable to various freshwater ecosystems, such as lakes, rivers, and reservoirs, providing essential baseline data for identifying MP sources and assessing ecosystem-based risks. Procedures specific to each sample type are outlined based on previously established techniques used widely in sampling practices[3–5] and adapted specifically for this study. Extraction steps vary among sample types (surface water samples[6–8] ; sediment samples[9–11] ; fish samples[12] ). However, procedures from the filtration step onward, aimed at identifying and quantifying MPs, remain consistent across all sample groups. 

The steadily increasing global production and consumption of plastics since the 1950s has led to significant environmental pollution in both aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems.[13][,][14] Initially recognized by the scientific community between the 1960s and 1970s, this issue was formally identified as a global environmental concern by the late 1990s.[15] Plastics, valued for their ease of processing, low cost, and durability, have seen a substantial increase in use particularly over the last 50 years.[16][,][17] However, their inherent properties allow them to fragment into smaller particles, facilitating their widespread distribution across distant regions via marine currents and winds, leading to escalating levels of pollution.[17] Plastic pollution, especially from single-use items,[18] constitutes one of the most serious environmental threats to aquatic ecosystems.[19][,][20] In aquatic environments, MPs enter food chains affecting both large organisms such as fish and microscopic organisms, posing risks to human food safety as well as causing economic losses.[21–23]

## Innovation 

This protocol introduces an integrative approach for extracting and analyzing microplastics from three distinct freshwater matrices: surface water, benthic sediment, and fish gastrointestinal contents. While many previous protocols target a single sample type, this method harmonizes sample processing steps beyond initial extraction to allow standardized downstream analysis. It features matrix-specific optimizations for digestion efficiency (e.g., H2O2 and NaClO/KOH treatments), and addresses issues such as filter clogging, polymer degradation, and cross-contamination. The protocol enhances accuracy and reproducibility by including systematic procedural blanks and particle validation via μ-Raman spectroscopy. Furthermore, it offers modular adaptability for laboratories with different levels of instrumentation and field conditions, including alternatives for low water levels and minimal equipment scenarios. By streamlining methodology across sample types while preserving analytical rigor, this protocol supports comprehensive microplastic monitoring in freshwater ecosystems.

Under no circumstances should microplastic studies involving biotic sampling proceed without securing the appropriate institutional or governmental permissions where required.

Here, we describe steps for extracting and analyzing microplastics from surface water samples collected using a manta trawl. The protocol begins with field collection at designated freshwater locations, where water is filtered through a 330 μm mesh net to retain plastic particles. Samples are then transferred to clean laboratory containers, followed by chemical digestion using hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) to remove organic matter. A density separation step using potassium carbonate (K2CO3) solution is applied to isolate MPs from remaining debris. The sample is then vacuum filtered onto glass microfiber (GF/C) filters for subsequent analysis. This sequence ensures effective recovery of microplastics ≥330 μm, with occasional retention of smaller particles due to mesh clogging during sampling.

Here, we describe steps for the extraction and isolation of MPs from benthic freshwater sediment samples. Sediment is collected using a Van Veen grab and stored in clean containers prior to laboratory processing. Dried sediment is subjected to density separation with potassium carbonate (K2CO3) to isolate MPs from mineral and organic fractions. The supernatant containing floated particles is filtered through GF/C filters. This procedure enables recovery of plastic particles down to 1.2 μm, depending on the filtration capacity. The sediment-specific steps are optimized to minimize organic interference and ensure efficient recovery across a range of polymer densities.

Fish specimens are humanely euthanized and dissected to remove the entire digestive tract. All procedures are designed to prevent sample contamination and ensure reliable downstream analysis.

## EXPECTED OUTCOMES 

This protocol enables the quantitative and qualitative analysis of MPs in water, sediment, and fish digestive system samples. MPs are classified through microscopic analysis based on their physical characteristics, including color, shape (fiber, fragment, film, or sphere), and size. For polymer identification, μ-Raman spectroscopy is employed, with a target polymer confirmation rate of at least 70%, achieved by analyzing a minimum of 30 particles per sample. The protocol ensures that sample material is evenly distributed on GF/C filters, which facilitates accurate and efficient microscopic counting. Moreover, it is designed to maintain an inter-sample contamination rate of less than 5%, thereby ensuring high data integrity and reliability throughout the analytical process. 

## LIMITATIONS 

MP detection in this protocol relies heavily on optical techniques and the expertise of the analyst, making it particularly challenging to accurately characterize particles smaller than 100 μm. The digestion of organic material using hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) and sodium hypochlorite/potassium hydroxide (NaClO/KOH) may be insufficient for some biological tissues, potentially leading to gel formation or foaming, which can delay the analysis process. In samples with a high particle load, filter clogging can occur, significantly extending the analysis time. Microscopic analysis can also lead to misclassification, particularly for transparent and white MPs, such as films, increasing the risk of false identifications. Furthermore, polymer confirmation using Raman spectroscopy may be affected by spectral ambiguities caused by the presence of additive chemicals within plastic particles, complicating accurate identification. Finally, due to the inherent environmental heterogeneity in freshwater ecosystems, the distribution of MPs between samples may exhibit high variability, necessitating careful interpretation of the results.

## TROUBLESHOOTING 

## Problem 1: Incomplete digestion of macrophytes or large particles in water or sediment 

## samples 

Large organic materials, such as macrophytes or other bulky particles, may not completely degrade in the H2O2 solution, causing them to accumulate on the filter paper. This can obstruct the identification and analysis of MPs (related to water and sediment samples, Step 4, 17). 

## Potential solution 

If large organic matter is present, use sodium hypochlorite (NaClO) / potassium hydroxide (KOH) solution for secondary digestion. Heat the solution at a maximum of 60[◦] C. Alternatively, manually remove these particles with forceps (time consuming), and use a pressurized water pump to wash them, ensuring that any adhering MPs are transferred to the filter. 

## Problem 2: Leakage in the vacuum filtration system 

A leakage in the vacuum filtration system may cause MPs in the solution phase to escape, compromising data reliability (related to sediment, water and fish samples, Step 12, 4, 19). 

## Potential solution 

Replace or adjust the gasket of the manifold system. Ensure that the gasket is appropriately sized to provide a tight, leak-proof seal. 

## Problem 3: Fungal growth in Petri dishes due to moisture 

Moist or wet filter papers left in Petri dishes can promote fungal growth, contaminating the samples (related to water, sediment and fish samples, Step 5, 12, 19). 

## Potential solution 

Ensure that filter papers are completely dry before placing them in Petri dishes. Verify that Petri dishes are clean and dry after washing and acetone treatment. Store Petri dishes under dry, sterile conditions. 

## Problem 4: Incomplete lipid digestion in fish gut samples 

Lipids in fish tissues may not dissolve completely in the H2O2 solution, slowing down the filtration process (related to fish samples, Step 17). 

## Potential solution 

Add an additional volume of H2O2 to the digestion solution and incubate for an additional 48 h at a maximum of 60[◦] C. 

## Problem 5: Gel formation due to prolonged lipid digestion in fish samples 

Extended incubation of fish tissues in H2O2 may cause gel formation, which can adhere to the manifold walls and trap MP particles (related to fish samples, Step 17). 

## Potential solution 

Minimize the time the solution remains in the manifold or filtration funnel. Ensure rapid transfer and filtration of the solution. 

## Problem 6: Filter clogging during filtration 

Excessive particle accumulation can block the filter, significantly slowing down the filtration process (related to fish samples, Step 19). 

## Potential solution 

Gradually transfer the dissolved solution into the filtration funnel, considering its viscosity and particle density. For highly concentrated solutions, split the sample into 2–3 portions for filtration.

## Problem 7: Foaming and overflow during heating on hot plate 

Solution may foam and overflow during heating, leading to the loss of MP particles (related to fish samples, Step 17). 

## Potential solution 

Monitor the solutions on the hot plate at regular intervals (e.g., hourly). Gently swirl the solution to maintain homogeneity, or add distilled water to maintain volume.

## Potential solution 

Complete the filtration process using the minimum necessary volume of solution and in the shortest possible time. 

## Problem 9: Uneven distribution of particles on filter paper 

Excessive particle accumulation on the filter can prevent even distribution and hinder microscopic analysis (related to fish samples, Step 19). 

## Potential solution 

Gradually add the dissolved solution to the filtration funnel, adjusting for solution viscosity and density. If necessary, divide the sample into 2–3 portions and filter each separately.

Petri dishes containing MP particles may tip over during transfer, causing particle loss (related to Step 20, 21, 22). 

## Potential solution 

Store Petri dishes with the lids facing downward in a stable container. Use a secure, compartmentalized storage box for transport. 

## Problem 11: Crystallization of K2CO3 on filter paper 

K2CO3 may crystallize on the filter paper, obscuring MP identification during microscopic analysis (related to sediment samples, Step 9). 

## Potential solution 

Ensure the K2CO3 solution is well-mixed before use. After filtering the sample, rinse the filter with a small amount of filtered water to minimize crystallization.
