## **1. Scope and Applicability** 

- The purpose of this document is to describe how source and treated drinking water samples should be collected on-site at drinking water treatment facilities for subsequent microplastic (MP) analysis using Raman or FTIR microscopy. 

- The procedure describes the assembly, operation, and disassembly of in-line holders which contain filters used to collect microplastics. Extraction of microplastics from filters, as well as additional processing methods (typically required) prior to analysis are discussed elsewhere. Inclusion of such methods is highly recommended in order to reduce the presence of extraneous, non-plastic particulates which may complicate the characterization of polymer types and sizes. 

## **2. Summary of Method** 

Samples containing microplastics are collected using an enclosed, in-line filtration equipment whereby membrane filters of decreasing pore sizes (e.g., 20 µm followed by 5 µm) are arranged in-series. The equipment is typically connected directly to sampling taps at drinking water treatment (DWT) facilities. The method is also applicable to other sampling point configurations provided that appropriate fittings, adapters, or reducers are available. A booster pump may be required to increase line pressure at the point of sampling. When using a booster pump, a constant head tank (that also serves as an air gap) may be required. Prior to sampling, individual equipment components are cleaned using pre-filtered surfactant, assembled, and sealed within a Class II laminar flow hood/biological safety cabinet (BSC). Filtration equipment is unsealed only immediately prior to direct connection to on-site sampling taps via a metal hose. Following filtration of a target volume of water, connections to the in-line filter holders are re-sealed and returned to a laminar flow hood for disassembly (removal of the filters and rinsing of internal components). A training video that illustrates this method is available at https://vimeo.com/1063685718 (password: 3535).

## **4. Health & Safety Warnings** 

- Follow all appropriate site-specific safety and PPE requirements when conducting sampling. 

- Equipment may be heavy – lift with care. 

- Cotton lab coats, safety glasses, and nitrile gloves should be worn when conducting all laboratory work. 

- Do not work with volatile or flammable materials in a laminar flow hood/BSC. 

## **5. Contamination Control** 

Microplastics represent a widespread atmospheric contaminant and are likely to be present in laboratory spaces. Measures to prevent contamination of samples are required, including the following: 

- Reduce exposure of sampling equipment to airborne microplastics. 

   - Assemble and disassemble in-line filter holders in a Class II laminar flow hood/BSC equipped with a HEPA filter. 

   - Turn on the Class II laminar flow hood/BSC at least 1 h prior to use. 

   - Perform all lab-related work in a Class II laminar flow hood/BSC whenever possible (except when working with materials which dictate the use of a fume hood) 

2 

to reduce the potential for airborne contamination. This includes activities such as vacuum filtration, rinsing, and sampling equipment preparation. 

   - Cover all glassware and bottles with aluminum foil (instead of plastic caps) when not in use. 

   - Where possible, adopt ISO Class 5 (or better) clean room guidelines. Employ HEPA filters throughout laboratory spaces and conduct work in dedicated, closed rooms. 

- Reduce/limit the use of plastic materials. 

   - Use glass or metal laboratory equipment (beakers, pipettes, etc.) whenever possible. 

   - When polymer materials must be used (i.e., membrane filters, pipette tips) use the same polymer type (if possible) and record all polymers used. These polymers may be excluded from subsequent analysis to avoid potential microplastic overestimation. 

   - If plastic materials are used, inspect their integrity. Blanks are analyzed to identify procedural contamination from plastics that may come in contact with the sample during processing. 

   - Purple nitrile gloves are associated with minimal contamination potential; their color makes it easy to distinguish if any contamination can be attributed to them. 

   - Be aware that polymeric membrane filters may degrade and shed particles during handling. 

- Prepare all rinsing solutions (e.g., ultrapure water and surfactant solution) immediately prior to use using microplastics analysis-grade (MAG) water. MAG water may be generated by filtering Type I (ultrapure) or II ASTM water  through a ≤1 µm filter (material such as cellulose acetate or polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE)) using a glass vacuum filtration apparatus in a Class II laminar flow hood (See Section 7.1). 

- Triple rinse all glassware and equipment with filtered surfactant solution prior to use.

- _7.1 Preparation of 0.05% MP-free Tween® 20 surfactant solution_ 

Note: Conduct all work in a Class II laminar flow hood/BSC. 

1. Prepare a 10% w/w Tween® or equivalent surfactant solution using Type I ASTM/ultrapure water. 

   - 1.1. Dilute the solution in Type I or II ASTM water to achieve a surfactant concentration of 0.05% (e.g., add 5 mL 10% w/w Tween® in 995 mL Type I or II ASTM water). 

   - 1.2. Rinse the vacuum filtration equipment by passing approximately 100 mL of 0.05% surfactant solution and rinsing the filtration flask into a waste beaker. Rinse the flask two additional times (total three rinses). 

2. Filter the remaining ~800 mL of 0.05% surfactant solution through a  0.45  m pore size filter membrane using the rinsed glass vacuum filtration equipment. 

3. Clean a storage bottle or beaker by triple rinsing with filtered 0.05% surfactant. 

8 

4. Pour remaining contents of the filtration flask into a cleaned bottle or beaker and store in a Class II laminar flow hood/BSC covered with aluminum foil. Use within one week of preparation. 

## _7.2 Cleaning_ 

1. Place the filter holders and associated internal components into a stainless-steel container filled with filtered 0.05% surfactant. 

   - 1.1. Open globe valves. 

   - 1.2. Ensure all quick connections and globe valves are fully submerged. 

2. Sonicate for 10 minutes at 400 kHz and 600 W. 

3. Subsequently rinse each component with ~50 mL additional filtered 0.05% surfactant. 

4. Equipment may be used immediately for sampling. However, if equipment is to be stored, air dry in a Class II laminar flow hood (typically several hours). 

## _7.3 In-line filter equipment assembly_ 

Note: Conduct all work in a Class II laminar flow hood/BSC. 

1. Triple rinse all materials (beakers, forceps, etc.) and equipment with filtered 0.05% surfactant solution. 

2. Fill a 50 mL beaker or flask with filtered 0.05% surfactant. 

3. Triple rinse a disposable 5 cm (2 in) glass Pasteur pipette and/or 100 mL beaker with filtered 0.05% surfactant. 

4. Store Pasteur pipettes and forceps submerged in a beaker containing filtered 0.05% surfactant to prevent potential airborne contamination. 

5. Gather all materials for filtration equipment as described in Section 6.2. 

6. Assemble filter holders starting with outlet components working towards inlet components. 

7. Rinse inside of the bottom half of the downstream filter holder 3 times with ~50 mL filtered 0.05% surfactant using a Pasteur pipette, or by pouring from a glass beaker with the globe valve open. Make sure to rinse all in internal areas including quick connections and globe valves. 

8. Close the globe valve and place the rinsed filter holder half facing upright. 

9 

9. Rinse the coarse filter support 3 times using ~50 mL filtered 0.05% surfactant solution. Hold the filter support using stainless steel forceps and place into the rinsed bottom half of the filter holder. 

10. Repeat rinsing for the fine filter support. 

11. Using stainless steel forceps, place a 47 mm diameter filter on the fine filter support (e.g., 5  m for downstream filter, 20  m for upstream filter). The filter does not need to be rinsed with surfactant. 

12. Rinse and apply the rubber O-ring on top of the membrane filter as per Step 9. 

13. Rinse the top half of the filter holder as per Step 7 (note that there is no globe valve between filter holders, only at the inlet/outlet ends of the entire filter system). 

14. Repeat Steps 6-13 for the upstream filter holder. 

15. Join the upstream and downstream filter holders by linking the outlet quick connection of the upstream filter to the inlet quick connection of the downstream filter. 

16. Ensure globe valves are closed; cover the ends of the in-line filter assembly with aluminum foil.

1. Connect a flexible stainless-steel hose to a sampling tap at a DWT facility using a ¼” quick connection. 

   - 1.1. Note: If a ¼” quick connection is not installed on the sampling tap, replace/add a ¼” quick connection to the tap, sealing threaded connections with PTFE tape (Figure 4a).

2. Check the line pressure using an in-line pressure gauge. If pressure reading is: 2.1. >50 psi for all desired sampling locations within a facility: proceed to Section 7.3.1

3. Note: Some DWT facilities may require a certified backflow preventor. Check with facility personnel to determine if the air gap provided by the constant head tank may provide sufficient backflow prevention. 

## _7.4.1 Direct connection/Sufficient line pressure (>50 psi)_ 

1. Connect a 50 psi PRV to the end of the stainless-steel tubing. Ensure that the PRV is rated to reduce the maximum line pressure (e.g., for the ¾” inlet PRV (adapted to ¼”) as shown in Figure 2b, typical maximum inlet pressure is approximately 300 psi). 

2. Flush for at least 15 minutes with sample water. 

   - 2.1. Ensure temperature and turbidity remain consistent within  1°C and  0.5 NTU, respectively, over a 5-minute period, flush longer if required. 

3. Uncover the outlet end of the in-line sampling equipment and quickly connect to wither a flow totalizer, or direct the effluent to a graduated cylinder, as appropriate. 

4. Uncover the inlet end of the sampling equipment and connect it to the outlet of the PRV (described in Step 1 above). 

5. Open the inlet valve followed by the outlet valve. 

6. Proceed to filter water through the sampling equipment. 

11 

   - 6.1. Sample volumes are typically dictated by: 1) collection of an appropriate volume as described by the subsequent analytical method (to ensure an appropriate number of particulates are collected), and 2) the volume which may be passed prior to occlusion of the filter pores (i.e., flow stops or becomes impractically slow). 

   - 6.2. Suggested sample volumes are 0.5-1 L for source water, 10-20 L for treated water. 

   - 6.3. Preliminary trials may be required to determine appropriate sample volumes. 

   - 6.4. If flowrates fall below the operational range of the flow totalizer being used, a graduated cylinder placed at the outlet of the sampling apparatus may be used to measure sample volume.  Always record the volume of filtered water. 

   - 6.5. The volume of water which may be filtered through the sampling apparatus will vary for different source waters (depending on turbidity, particle size distribution, etc.). Always accurately record the volume of water filtered through the sampling equipment. For highly turbid water (e.g., >5-10 NTU, where filtration of <1 L may not be possible) or in the case of low-flow situations (below the operational minimum of a flow totalizer), a graduated cylinder placed at the outlet may serve as the most convenient and accurate way to measure volume. 

7. Close the inlet, immediately followed by the outlet globe valves. 

8. Disconnect and cover the open ends with aluminum foil. 

9. Note: an in-line pressure transducer or in-line pressure gauge may be used to monitor filter integrity (i.e., watch for any sudden decrease in line pressure). Rinse any in-line transducers or gauges upstream of the sampling apparatus for 15 minutes before use as per Step 2 above. 

## _7.4.2 Use of booster pump/Insufficient line pressure (<50 psi)_ 

1. Prior to use, triple rinse the interior of the constant head tank (CHT, reservoir) as well as its lid with at least 1 L of sample water and cover. 

2. Attach inlet, overflow, and outlet stainless steel hoses to the CHT (reservoir). 

3. Connect the CHT outlet tube to the pump inlet; connect a stainless-steel tube with a 50 psi PRV to the pump outlet. 

4. Allow the CHT (reservoir) to fill to a level higher than the outlet port. Monitor the CHT level using the overflow hose while the end of the hose is held above the overflow port. If water 

12 

flows from the elevated overflow hose, this indicates water is rising above the overflow port, and the inlet flow into the CHT should be reduced. 

5. Turn the pump on and flush the system for 15 minutes. 

   - 5.1. Ensure temperature and turbidity are consistent within  1°C and  0.5 NTU, respectively, over a 5-minute period, flush longer if required. 

6. Collect sample as per Section 7.3.1 steps 3-9 above. 

## _7.5 Sample blanks_ 

Sample blanks may be employed to evaluate potential microplastic contamination associated with the overall sampling procedure. Prepare blank filter holder(s) in the same manner as those used for sampling including all assembly, disassembly, and cleaning steps (described in Sections 7.3, 7.6, and 7.2). Take the blank filters on-site but do not connect to a sampling line. Remove and replace the aluminum foil and open the globe valves to the atmosphere for as long as it would normally take to connect to the inlet stainless steel hose. This procedure is designed to expose the sampling equipment to potential airborne microplastics. Filtration of MAG water or equivalent is not required. 

## _7.6 In-line filter holder disassembly_ 

Note: Conduct all work in a Class II laminar flow hood/BSC. 

1. Rinse all glassware and equipment as described Section 7.3.1 to 7.3.3. Store forceps and Pasteur pipettes submerged in a beaker filled with filtered 0.05% surfactant solution. 

2. Rinse the exterior of the filter holder assembly with filtered 0.05% surfactant using a 5 cm (2 in) glass Pasteur pipette or by pouring from a glass beaker to remove potential contamination which may have occurred during handling. 

3. Holding the filter assembly upright, remove the aluminum foil, open the upstream then downstream globe valves to drain excess water retained in the equipment. 

   - 3.1. A vacuum pump may be used to assist if water does not drain easily (i.e., for samples containing many particles). Connect the vacuum to the bottom globe valve using a ¼” NPT quick connection. The vacuum pressure must not exceed 50 psi. 

4. Close the globe valves. 

5. Disconnect the upstream and downstream filter holders. Make sure the filter holders are always held in an upright position using a retort stand and clamp to prevent any potential particle loss. 

13 

6. Unscrew and remove the bolts that hold the stainless-steel filter holders together, taking care not to move the filter holder plates. 

7. Remove the top half of the filter holder by lifting directly up. 

8. Rinse the interior of the top filter holder 3 times into a clean beaker using ~50 mL filtered 0.05% surfactant solution by pouring from a clean beaker or delivered using a Pasteur pipette. 8.1. Make sure to rinse inside the quick connection and globe valve. 

9. Using stainless steel forceps, carefully lift the O-ring and rinse 3 times with filtered 0.05% surfactant into a 500-600 mL beaker. 

10. Using forceps, carefully slide the filter horizontally into the 500-600 mL beaker, making sure that the filter does not fold. 

11. Rinse the fine and coarse filter support screens as per Step 8. 

12. Rinse the interior of the bottom half of the filter holder 3 times into the beaker using 50 mL filtered 0.05% surfactant solution, making sure to rise the interior of the quick connection outlet. 

13. Repeat Steps 6-12 for the downstream filter holder excluding the bottom half of the filter holder, rinsing into the same 500-600 mL beaker. 

   - 13.1. Note: Do not rinse the bottom half of the downstream filter holder (i.e., below the lower pore size filter) as this will be associated with a lower particle size threshold. 

   - 13.2. Note: At this point, the 500-600 mL beaker will contain the rinse water from all components of the sampling equipment, including upstream and downstream filter holders and both filters, representing the sample that may be subsequently digested to remove nonplastic particulates and analyzed using spectroscopic techniques. 

14. Label beaker and cover with aluminum foil. 

15. Rinse all glassware and equipment (forceps, Pasteur pipettes, beakers) with filtered 0.05% surfactant during disassembly of the sampling equipment. 

16. Clean the equipment as per Section 7.2.

## **8. Troubleshooting** 

## _8.1 Rapid clogging_ 

For samples associated with treatment processes upstream of full-scale filtration at a DWT facility (i.e., raw water, flocculation tank, post-sedimentation), the in-line filters may clog quickly, before a desired volume of water may be collected. 

1. Adjust sample volume 

   - 1.1. For raw water or any sample collected prior to full-scale filtration, where particulates are most abundant, sample volumes are typically lower than collected volumes for post-filter samples. 

2. Adjust membrane filter pore sizes. 

   - 2.1. Upstream filter pore size (larger or smaller) may be required to reduce occlusion of the subsequent smaller pore size filter. 

   - 2.2. Note: The smallest pore size filter will dictate the smallest size of particle that can be analyzed. 

3. Consider measuring filtered water with a graduated cylinder at the outlet of the sampling apparatus, as the flow rate may drop to below the operational range of many commercially available flow totalizers. 

4. If unusual clogging of a filter occurs, check for wrinkles or defects on the filter surface. If any defects are identified, replace with a new filter. 

Note: Always open filter holders in a Class II laminar flow hood/BSC. Do not open filter holders on-site as this will compromise the sample. 

## _8.2 Leaking_ 

1. Tighten the filter holder bolts on-site. 

2. Always carry spare sample filtration equipment that is ready to use. 

3. Make sure all threaded connections are tightened; use PTFE tape (as required) to prevent leaks.

## **10. Quality Assurance and Quality Control** 

Incorporation of blanks/negative controls (as described in Section 7.4) are recommended as microplastics are ubiquitous and many laboratory materials are comprised of plastic. In addition, polymeric membrane filters may potentially shed particles during handling. As the in-line sampling method represents a closed system, process/in-lab blanks (as described in Standard Operating Procedures for Extraction and Measurement by Raman Spectroscopy of Microplastic Particles in Drinking Water, SWB-MP2-rev1) may be required to quantify any particles that may be introduced during sample handling. It is suggested that one blank be included per sampling event or, every 10 samples.
