## 29 **Abstract** 

- 30 In the literature, there is widespread consensus that methods in plastic research need 31 improvement. Current limitations in quality assurance and harmonization prevent progress in 

- 32 our understanding of what the true effects of microplastic in the environment are. Following 

- 33 the recent development of quality assessment methods for studies reporting concentrations in 

- 34 biota and water samples, we propose a method to assess the quality of microplastic effect 35 studies. We reviewed 105 microplastic effect studies with aquatic biota, provided a systematic 

- 36 overview of their characteristics, developed 20 quality criteria in four main criteria categories 

- 37 (particle characterization, experimental design, applicability in risk assessment, and ecological 

- 38 relevance), propose a protocol for future effect studies with particles, and, finally, used all the 

- 39 information to define the weight of evidence with respect to demonstrated effect mechanisms. 

- 40 On average, studies scored 44.6% (range 20-77.5%) of the maximum score. No study scored 

- 41 positively on all criteria, reconfirming the urgent need for better quality assurance. Most urgent 

- 42 recommendations for improvement relate to avoiding and verifying background contamination, 

- 43 and to improving the environmental relevance of exposure conditions. The majority of the 44 studies (86.7%) evaluated on particle characteristics properly, nonetheless it should be 45 underlined that by failing to provide characteristics of the particles, an entire experiment can 46 become irreproducible. Studies addressed environmentally realistic polymer types fairly well; 47 however, there was a mismatch between sizes tested and those targeted when analyzing 

- 48 microplastic in environmental samples. In far too many instances, studies suggest and speculate 

- 49 mechanisms that are poorly supported by the design and reporting of data in the study. This 

- 50 represents a problem for decision-makers and needs to be minimized in future research. In their 

- 51 papers, authors frame 10 effects mechanisms as ‘suggested’, whereas 7 of them are framed as 

- 52 ‘demonstrated’. When accounting for the quality of the studies according to our assessment, 

- 53 three of these mechanisms remained. These are _inhibition of food assimilation and/or decreased_

- 54 _nutritional value of food_ , _internal physical damage_ and _external physical damage_ . We 

- 55 recommend that risk assessment addresses these mechanisms with higher priority.

- 57 **Introduction** 

- 58 In the last decade, the body of literature addressing the occurrence and impacts of plastic debris 

- 59 has substantially increased.[1] Particular attention has been given to microplastic particles (MP), 

- 60 generally defined as plastic particles 1 µm - 5 mm[2–7] which have been detected at a wide range 

- 61 of concentrations in various aquatic systems, from remote marine to coastal zone and estuarine 

- 62 areas, as well as in freshwater lakes and rivers.[8–11] Their ubiquity in aquatic systems and their 

- 63 small size has resulted in concerns regarding their effects on aquatic biota for which ingestion 64 has been observed at all levels of biological organization.[12–14] 

- 65 Characterizing and quantifying the environmental fate and transport of MP requires insight into 

- 66 the influence of various environmental processes and pathways.[8,15,16] The release of MP into 

- 67 the environment can occur either directly, such as via primary emissions from products during 

- 68 their manufacture and consumer-use life cycle, or alternatively, can be generated from the 69 degradation and fragmentation of mismanaged plastic waste, commonly referred to as 

- 70 secondary MP, which results in a heterogeneous mixture of particle types, shapes, and sizes 

- 71 released to the environment.[17] It is generally agreed that secondary sources represent the 

- 72 dominant source of MP.[18] Primary sources are estimated to contribute between 15 and 31% of 73 all plastic in the environment.[19] 

- 74 To assess the ecological risk associated with exposure to MP, there is a need to develop robust 

- 75 toxicological dose-response relationships, which can effectively relate environmentally 

- 76 relevant exposures with effects.[20] Because of the heterogeneous presence of MP in the 

- 77 environment of varying concentrations of shapes, sizes, and polymer composition, there is a

- 103 relevant exposures.[20,44] Consequently, the weight of the evidence supportive of a quantitative 

- 104 risk assessment for MP remains unclear. Recent reviews have discussed the evidence regarding 

- 105 the occurrence of MP effects and the underlying effect mechanisms.[45–47] However, in their 

- 106 evaluations of the literature, the quality of studies was not taken into account, possibly leading 

- 107 to biased assessments. While these reviews underline that the quality of effect studies should 

- 108 improve, and call for more ecologically and environmentally relevant exposure systems in order 

- 109 to better assess the effect of MP on the environment, we argue that the quality of studies should 

- 110 be assessed first, in order to be able to discard unreliable data. 

- 111 A fundamental element of assessing ecological risk is the availability of a suite of standardized 

- 112 test systems and analytical tools and methods, which enable the application of dose-response 

- 113 relationships relating environmental exposure to effect threshold concentrations that are 

- 114 consistent and of sufficient quality.[48–50] This also applies to the relatively young field of MP 

- 115 risk assessment, where many studies have emphasized the need to improve the quality of data

- 117 studies reporting on exposure concentrations of MPs in biota and in surface and drinking water, 

- 118 adopting methods similar to the existing Klimisch and CRED approaches[48,49] , have recently 

- 119 been developed and applied.[54,55] Whereas these systems and aspects of these systems start to be 

- 120 adopted and recommended in the literature,[59–66] currently, a similar evaluation method for 

- 121 assessing the quality of MP effect studies is lacking.

- 124 effects of MP on aquatic biota, emphasizing quality assurance aspects of studies, and assessing 

- 125 the weight of the evidence (WOE) the studies provide with respect to the effect mechanisms

- 136 **2. Methods** 

- 137 _2.1 Literature search_ 

- 138 Literature was retrieved from the database from the systematic review underlying the SAPEA 

- 139 report.[1] In addition, an extensive literature search accessing the Natural Science Collection 

- 140 database available at ProQuest[®] was performed for ecotoxicological effect studies with MP 

- 141 until November 2019. The following search strings were used: (effect OR impact OR endpoint 

- 142 OR toxicity) AND (growth OR feeding OR consumption OR survival OR mortality OR 143 behavior OR behaviour OR stress OR response(s) OR activity OR reproduction OR inhibition) 

- 144 AND (microplastic(s) OR microbead OR polyethylene (PE) OR polystyrene (PS) OR 145 polyamide (PA) OR polypropylene (PP) OR polyvinyl chloride (PVC)) AND (aquatic OR 

- 146 freshwater OR marine OR estuarine) NOT (chemicals OR additives). Studies were only 

- 147 included when at least one type of MP tested had a diameter between 1 _µ_ m and 5 mm. To enable 

- 148 interpretation of particle effects, studies explicitly aiming to study effects of plastic-associated 

- 149 chemicals, or aiming to solely study accumulation, ingestion and/or egestion of MP were 150 excluded from the analysis. 

- 151 _2.2 Assessment of general study characteristics_

- 152 A total of 10 characteristics were extracted from each paper and summarized (see Table S1 in 

- 153 the Supporting Information): Size, Shape, Polymer type, Ecosystem (fresh, marine, estuarine), 

- 154 Taxonomy categories (Class, Species), Exposure duration, Endpoints studied, Endpoints 

- 155 affected and Effect threshold when reported (as either LCx, ECx, LOEC or NOEC). When a 

- 156 size range was used, the upper and lower size ranges are noted, however, if an average size was 

- 157 provided together with the range, the average is also recorded. In instances when the average 

- 158 was not given, it is assumed that the particles are uniformly distributed between the upper and 

- 159 lower size limit and that the average can be estimated accordingly. For shapes, the terms 

- 160 “beads” and “spheres” are assumed to be the same and are combined in a single category. As 

- 161 the definition of “irregular” is ambiguous and could include any non-regular shape, it is 162 included as a separate category. 

- 163 For the analysis of the taxonomic groups we followed De Sá et al. (2018), where classes 164 polychaeta and clitellata are combined in the category “annelida”, classes bivalvia and 

- 165 gastropoda are combined in the category “mollusca”, classes anthozoa and hydrozoa are

- 172 _2.3 Quantitative quality assessment_ 

- 173 All of the 105 reviewed studies are evaluated based on 20 Quality assurance/Quality control 174 (QA/QC) criteria in the following categories: particle characterization, experimental design, 

- 175 applicability for risk assessment, and ecological relevance. These categories are consistent with 

ACS Paragon Plus Environment 

Page 8 of 44 

Environmental Science & Technology 

- 176 the principles of sound ecotoxicology proposed by Harris et al. (2014), which represent 

- 177 fundamental elements for ensuring quality and reproducibility and are thus critical when 

- 178 designing, applying and reporting ecotoxicological effect studies for MP.[67] A summary of the 

- 179 20 QA/QC criteria is shown in Table 1 and a detailed motivation for each criterion is provided 

- 180 in the Supporting Information (see methods continued). Building on the methods developed by 

- 181 both Hermsen et al. (2018) and Koelmans et al., (2019), each criterion is assigned a score of 

- 182 either 2 (adequate), 1 (adequate with restrictions) or 0 (inadequate) points (see Table S2).[54,55] 

- 183 All studies collated as part of this literature review are independently assessed by three of the 

- 184 authors, with scores subsequently tabulated and discussed to reach consensus, sometimes 185 leading to adjustments of the original formulation of a criterion to decrease potential 186 ambiguities. The scores per individual study are provided in the Supporting Information (see 187 Table S3). 

- 188 Consistent with the approach adopted in previous method evaluation papers,[54,55] we emphasize 

- 189 that the scores assigned for each study should not be perceived as a judgement indicative of the 190 relative value of a study, i.e. a paper scoring low on a certain criterion could still provide 191 valuable and reliable information regarding other potential insights. Problem formulation is 192 therefore an important element to understand, in that depending on the purpose of an effect 

- 193 study the results may or may not help to inform the decision-making process with respect to 

- 194 assessing risk. A WOE may be assembled, for instance, regarding an effect mechanism, but the 

- 195 mechanism may not necessarily be ecologically relevant (see SI, criterion 13 Endpoints, p11, 

- 196 methods continued). The primary objective of the evaluation criteria developed and applied in 

- 197 this study is directed at providing insight regarding those aspects of MP ecotoxicological effect 

- 198 studies that could be improved in future studies in order to better inform the application of a 

- 199 quantitative environmental risk assessment. The evaluation criteria, however, also provide the 

- 200 opportunity to assess the current WOE of effect mechanisms.

- 201 _2.4 Analysis of perceived versus demonstrated mechanisms explaining adverse effects_ 

- 202 Authors’ conclusions with respect to observed adverse effects and the mechanisms explaining 

- 203 them are summarized in the Supporting Information (see Table S3). In instances where the 

- 204 discussion and conclusions included ambiguous terms, such as, ‘may’, ‘could’, ‘can’, ‘would’, 

- 205 ‘postulate’, ‘suggest’, ‘might’, ‘potentially’, ‘most likely’, ‘imply’ the reported mechanisms are 

- 206 classified under the category ‘suggested’. If the discussion and/or conclusion used more 

- 207 definitive terminology, such as, ‘demonstrate’, ‘observe’, ‘indicate’, ‘induce’, ‘provide’, and 

- 208 ‘evidence’, the reported mechanisms are classified under the category ‘demonstrated’. When a 

- 209 combination of both ambiguous and definitive terminology are used in the same sentence to 

- 210 describe an effect mechanism, the mechanism is considered as ‘suggested’. Terms that imply a 

- 211 mechanism to be either ‘demonstrated’ or ‘speculated’ are reported in italic, whereas key words 

- 212 indicating the mechanism category are reported in bold. Finally, in addition to classifying effect 

- 213 mechanisms as either ‘‘suggested’ or ‘demonstrated’, specific categories based on the modes 

- 214 of actions proposed by authors are recorded and numbered accordingly. 

- 215 **3. Results and discussion** 

216 _**3.1. Study characteristics**_ 

- 217 _3.2.1 Characteristics of the tested microplastics: size, shape, polymer type_ 

- 218 _Size -_ A total of 178 different MP sizes have been tested in the 105 reviewed papers. The 

- 219 cumulative distribution illustrates that about 75% of studies tested the effects for MP <100 _µ_ m, 

- 220 or ‘small MPs’[55,66] (Figure 1), with approximately 30% of MP having sizes <10 µm. Of the 

- 221 178 sizes tested, 58.4% corresponded to a size range, while 41.6% consisted of one size only. 

- 222 Moreover, 16.3% of the tested MP included a size range greater than one order of magnitude. 

ACS Paragon Plus Environment 

Page 10 of 44 

Environmental Science & Technology 

- 223 Species-specific traits, such as size selective ingestion of MP have been demonstrated for 

- 224 aquatic organisms.[25,38,68,69] Size selectivity can potentially help understanding effect 

- 225 mechanisms that influence the toxicological response of an organism. Mechanistic insight, 

- 226 however, can only be demonstrated when an appropriate range of particles sizes is used. 

- 227 Therefore, when evaluating the effects of MP of only one size, the most detrimental sizes for a 

- 228 specific species may not be included in the analysis, resulting in an underestimation of actual 

- 229 effects across the MP size range.  Furthermore, it can be assumed that effects of MP of a certain 

- 230 size will differ in the presence of other sizes of MP, since there can be complex particle-particle 

- 231 interactions that may influence exposure as well as complex organism-particle interactions that 

- 232 can be difficult to account for when limiting testing to one size or narrow size range 233 distributions. The observation that effects testing of MP to date is dominated by particles < 100 

- 234 _µ_ m (Figure 1) implies that comparisons between MP sizes used in effect studies and sizes of 

- 235 MP found in the environment are difficult to be made, particularly since the detection of MP 

- 236 <100 _µ_ m represents an ongoing analytical challenge.[55] Nevertheless, we recommend the use of 

- 237 MP size distributions that are appropriate for the species being tested, which can potentially 

- 238 add greater insight between adverse effects and organism-particle interactions. 

- 239 _Shape -_ The shapes of MP reported in the 105 studies are dominated by spheres/beads, followed 

- 240 by irregular MP, fragments and fibers (Figure 2A). We assume that most of the studies reporting 

- 241 the use of irregular MP have tested either fragments, films, foams and sheets, or a combination 

- 242 thereof. Consequently, characterizing MP into distinct categories includes a subjective, 

- 243 qualitative, element that is difficult to enable differentiation, but which could result in greater 

- 244 refinement of shapes divided into more categories that would provide opportunities for better 245 mechanistic understanding. 

- 246 When comparing the shapes used in different effect studies with those shapes commonly 

- 247 observed in environmental samples, there is considerable inconsistency. While 58.1% of effect 

ACS Paragon Plus Environment 

Page 11 of 44 

Environmental Science & Technology 

- 248 studies have tested MP spheres/beads, this category only represents 6.5% of the MP detected in 

- 249 water and sediment samples.[17] In contrast, only 8.1% of the tested MP in effects studies were 

- 250 fibers, although they are the most abundant shape category detected in water and sediment, 

- 251 typically representing about half of MP detected.[17] Therefore, the use of fibers in effects studies 

- 252 represents a significant opportunity for advancing quantitative data for the purposes of assessing 253 environmental risks. 

- 254 An important factor to consider in future studies is how the shape of MP might influence their 255 ingestion and egestion by aquatic organisms,[40,70,71] which can potentially influence their relative 256 toxicity. Thus, the use of shapes representative of those detected in the environment has the 257 potential to benefit both the ecological relevance and mechanistic understanding of risks 258 associated with MP commonly encountered in the environment. 

- 259 _Polymer type –_ The most common polymer types used in the 105 effect studies reviewed were 260 PS and PE. Together they represent 62.3% of the MP types tested (Figure 2B). The use of these 

- 261 two polymers is relatively consistent with the polymer types typically observed in the 262 environment, whereby the three most commonly detected polymers in surface waters are PS, 263 PE, and PP.[55] In effect studies, however, the inclusion of PP is limited to only 5.5% of MPs 264 tested. Given that the polymer type can influence the fate of MP in both the test system and 265 ecosystem, depending on its density, surface chemistry, degree of crystallinity, and presence of 

- 266 chemical additives and plasticizers, it is important to include as much detail as possible with 

- 267 respect to the polymer composition.[16,20] Consistent with the need to advance the effects testing 

- 268 and mechanistic understanding of MP with respect to size and shape, as discussed above, there 

- 269 is also a need to strengthen understanding of the influence that the polymer composition may 

- 270 represent towards an observed adverse effect on various species. Insight regarding the 

- 271 relationships between size, shape and polymer composition is important for advancing 272 environmental risk assessment and helping to inform the decision-making process.

273 _3.2.2. Exposed organisms, exposure duration, endpoints studied, and effect thresholds reported._ 

- 274 The organisms tested in the 105 studies evaluated consist of 52.4% marine, 42.9% freshwater 275 and 4.8% estuarine species. The most abundant organisms studied are small crustaceans (which 

- 276 belong to the zooplankton category), followed by mollusks and fish (Figure 3A). The most 

- 277 common exposure durations used were; 24 h, 96 h, 240 h (10 days), 336 h (14 days), 504 h (21 

- 278 days) and 672 h (28 days) (see Figure S2 Supporting Information). The exposure durations 279 generally correspond to the recommended exposure durations of standard ecotoxicity test 280 guidelines for chemicals, implying that exposure durations are also closely linked to standard 281 effect endpoints, such as mortality, growth, and reproduction. However, there is literature 282 indicating that effects of MP can be time dependent[27,72,73] and standard test protocol guidelines 283 applicable for chemicals may not be applicable for the effect testing of MPs. Nevertheless, 284 chronic effects testing of MP adopting longer study durations does not appear to be well 285 represented, with only 18% of studies using an exposure time > 28 d, and < 2% (i.e. 2 papers) 286 with exposure times above three months. Consequently, it is recommended that future effects 287 testing include greater emphasis on assessing longer term effects. 

- 288 Effects of MP on growth are observed to be the most often studied (25.4%), followed by sub289 organismal endpoints (21.4%), survival (14.7%), feeding (11.5%), and reproduction (9.9%) 290 (Figure 3B). Population-level endpoints correspond to only <4% of the total endpoints studied. 

- 291 From the 105 papers, only about 10% reported effect thresholds (as either LCx, ECx, LOEC or 292 NOEC). Of all the studies providing effect thresholds, 33.3% report them as number 

- 293 concentration (i.e., particles/L), 50% as weight concentration (i.e., mg/L), and 16.7% in both 

- 294 units. In order to assess the environmental risks of MP, effect thresholds are fundamental, 

- 295 preferably in both units, which will also further enable comparisons between studies for use in 

- 296 developing quantitative WOE with respect to effects and risks.

## 297 _**3.2. Quality assessment**_ 

- 298 The results of the scoring based on the quantitative quality assessment proposed in this study 299 imply that substantial improvements can be made in how MP effect studies are designed and 300 conducted (Figures 4 and 5). As previously stated, the scores obtained should not be interpreted 301 as an absolute value of judgment, but as a guide for identifying and prioritizing study-design 302 components that would benefit most in improvement for the purposes of assessing 303 environmental risks. Consequently, we suggest that those studies with relatively high scores 304 represent the most reliable and useful in the context of risk assessment (Figure 5). Individual 305 studies, however, often had other objectives, which were not necessarily consistent with 306 information needed to support an assessment of risk. It is important, therefore, to assess each 307 of the specific criteria and to compare them with other studies rather than simply evaluating the 308 studies based on how they rank on their total score. The first subset of criteria (criteria 1 – 12) 309 enable the evaluation of the general technical quality of an effect test study. Here, the average 310 score across all studies is 11.3 (range 5 to 18), of a maximum possible score of 24. In this first 311 subset there are no studies for which positive scores on all quality criteria is assigned. The 312 second set of criteria (criteria 13 - 20) relates to the relevance of the papers for their use in 313 environmental risk assessment. For these criteria, the average score across all studies evaluated 314 is 6.6 (range 0 to 14) of a maximum potential score of 16. Again, no studies had positive scores 

- 315 for each of the ecological relevance quality criteria defined. Finally, the total scores combine 316 both the technical quality and ecological relevance evaluation criteria, whereby the total score 317 can be used as part of a quantitative WOE approach in the context of risk assessment. The 

- 318 average total score is 17.8 (range 8 to 31), from a maximum possible score of 40, indicating 

- 319 that results from effect studies assessing MP are often not fully reliable and/or reproducible. 

- 320 All studies included in this review were assigned a criterion value of 0 in at least one criterion, 

- 321 implying that important QA/QC criteria are consistently poorly addressed in the design and 

ACS Paragon Plus Environment 

Page 14 of 44 

Environmental Science & Technology 

- 322 reporting of MP effect studies. With respect to the general technical quality of the effect studies 

- 323 evaluated, 34.8% of the criteria in studies are assigned a value of 0, whereas 50.1% of studies 

- 324 receive the same poor quality score with respect to their ecological relevance. Average scores 

- 325 per criterion ranged from 0.06 to 1.79 (Figure 4). Those criteria that are typically evaluated high 

- 326 across all studies include the reporting of the source of the MP, the use of replicates, reporting 

- 327 on ecologically relevant endpoints and the inclusion of food particles within the test study. A 328 more detailed evaluation of each category is provided below. 

- 329 _Particle characterization -_ The category with the highest average score is “particle 330 characterization” (Figure 4). Overall, the majority of studies evaluated are observed to provide 331 satisfactory reporting on particle characteristics (scores >1). Only a limited number of studies 

- 332 (13.3%) fail to report on either one of these specifics. Improvements, however, are suggested, 

- 333 such as related to efforts towards the confirmation of size, shape and polymer type, as opposed 334 to simply relying on information from the manufacturer. Nonetheless, by failing to provide 

- 335 characteristics of the particles, an entire experiment can become irreproducible. Lastly, it should 336 be noted that approximately 60.0% of studies either don’t report a concentration or limit 

- 337 reporting to a mass or number concentration, which further complicates comparison across 338 studies. It is thus suggested that with relatively limited resource towards addressing the 339 shortcomings identified, substantial improvements can be realized within this quality criteria 340 category. 

- 341 _Experimental design -_ As a general observation, the majority of studies scored poorly within 

- 342 the category of experimental design (Figure 4). Concern is particularly apparent with respect to 

- 343 the quality evaluation criteria of “laboratory preparation” and “verification of background 

- 344 contamination”, with average scores of 0.18 and 0.06, respectively. While MP are often said to 

- 345 be ubiquitous in the environment, including indoor (laboratory) air,[1] only 3.8% of the reviewed 

- 346 studies thoroughly report how they minimized potential contamination arising from air, water 

ACS Paragon Plus Environment 

Page 15 of 44 

Environmental Science & Technology 

- 347 and all materials used during the experiment. Additionally, only 4.8% of the reviewed papers 348 verified the background contamination (visually). 

- 349 Only a few, 6.7%, of the evaluated studies included a protocol specifically used to pre-clean 

- 350 MP with an organic solvent. Additionally, 20% of studies took measures to ensure chemical 

- 351 purity. For instance, Karami et al. (2017) and Romano et al. (2018) measured certain chemical 

- 352 contaminants associated with the MP,[75] however, this still does not exclude chemical effects 

- 353 from experimental results. Some studies include a solvent control, but do not account for 

- 354 chemical contaminants that might be present in the MP themselves.[76] Importantly, the majority 

- 355 of studies (73.3%) do not mention the potential for chemical contaminants influencing observed 

- 356 adverse effects, making it difficult to disentangle particle toxicity from a potential chemical 357 toxicity. 

- 358 The criteria “verification of exposure” and “homogeneity of exposure” also are observed to 359 score low, with average scores of 0.45 and 0.68 (n = 105), respectively. These criteria are critical 

- 360 for enabling the reproducibility of study results, which further increase the uncertainty 361 associated with reported effect thresholds. Finally, the criterion “exposure assessment” 362 (average value of 0.84) is generally unsatisfactory in the studies evaluated. While most studies 

- 363 (78.1%) include a description verifying that MP have been ingested by test organisms, 

- 364 verification is often (72.4%) demonstrated in either a separate experiment, qualitatively, 365 visually or without a digestion step. 

- 366 While it is acknowledged that the resources needed to address the shortcomings identified with 

- 367 the criteria falling under the category of ‘experimental design’ are likely to be high, failing to 

- 368 address the various criterion results in studies with greater uncertainties and which thus fail to 

- 369 add value to broader scientific understanding as well as for strengthening opportunities to assess 

- 370 environmental risk. It is therefore prudent to carefully consider experimental design in future

- 371 effect studies, with the development and application of standard test protocols applicable to MP 

- 372 identified as an urgent need to better guide researchers. 

- 373 _Applicability to risk assessment –_ An important implication of data reported from ecotoxicity 

- 374 effects studies is their role in assessing environmental risks. Consequently, suggestions for 

- 375 improvement made under this category are perceived to have implications for the regulatory 

- 376 decision-making process. Results from the studies evaluated under the criteria related to 

- 377 applicability for risk assessment imply the need for improvements to “reporting of effect 

- 378 thresholds” and “quality of dose-response relationship”, where average scores of 0.25 and 0.48 

- 379 were assessed, respectively. As mentioned above, a limited number of studies (10.5%) are 

- 380 observed to explicitly report on effect thresholds with an indication of error. Moreover, only

## 395 _**3.3. WOE for mechanisms explaining adverse effects of microplastic on aquatic biota**_ 

- 396 Currently, the knowledge on effect mechanisms for MP is limited and there is a need to increase 

- 397 mechanistic understanding of toxicological modes-of-action.[79–81] Criterion #11 “exposure 

- 398 assessment of organism” aims at improving the strategic design of effect testing that might 

- 399 enable results to differentiate between intrinsic physicochemical properties of the MP 

- 400 themselves and how those interact with species-specific biological and physiological traits to 

- 401 influence an observed adverse effect (see Supporting Information, methods continued). 402 Acknowledging that MP represent a complex mixture of particles (shape, size and type), 403 incorporating strategies that enable effect-assessment to move from a ‘substance-based’ 404 approach to a ‘mechanism-based’ approach may add considerable value in assessing 405 environmental risk, not just for MP but for any other particle-stressor organisms may 406 encounter.[79,80] Knowledge on effect mechanisms will enhance the strategic application of 407 species sensitivity distributions for distinct categories of effects. Finally, advancing scientific 

- 408 understanding of particle effect mechanisms, such as those associated with exposure to MP, 409 will aid in the development of effect models.[82] 

- 410 Given the importance of advancing the scientific weight-of-evidence with respect to the effect 

- 411 mechanisms following exposure to MP, each of the 105 studies is reviewed with respect to the 

- 412 mechanisms that authors used to explain the adverse effects they observed. The analysis is 413 based on four considerations. Firstly, we verified whether authors refer to the mechanisms they 414 described using terms such as ‘suggested’ versus ‘demonstrated’ (see Table S3 in the 415 Supporting Information). If authors themselves described a mechanism as ‘demonstrated’, the 

- 416 WOE is perceived to be stronger. Secondly, the frequency of reporting certain mechanisms  was 

- 417 assessed (Table 2). The more often a mechanism is reported in the literature, the stronger the 

- 418 perceived WOE can be considered to be, in that consistency between studies in relation to 

- 419 observed effect mechanisms is assumed. Third, the relative strength of the WOE supportive of

- 420 an effect mechanism is further scrutinized based on the criteria #6 “chemical purity”, #14 

- 421 “addition of food” and most importantly #11 “exposure assessment of organism”. While all 20 

- 422 criteria are crucial in order to ensure quality and reproducibility of data from effect studies, the 

- 423 latter three criteria are specifically important in order to successfully assess the mechanisms 

- 424 behind adverse effects. Fourth and finally, the scores from the QA/QC assessment are used to 425 assess the relative credibility of effect mechanisms reported.

- 521 **Perspective and outlook** 

- 522 Research on the effects of MPs on biota in aquatic and other environmental compartments is a 

- 523 relatively new discipline in the environmental sciences. As a result, approaches to assess these 

- 524 effects vary widely across research groups, with both the nature of effects testing and analytical 

- 525 methods developing rapidly over time. Here, we evaluate the quality of 105 studies that report 

- 526 on the ecotoxicological effects of MPs for aquatic biota. The evaluation includes studies of 

- 527 organisms at various functional groups, such as phytoplankton, macrophytes, zooplankton, 

- 528 benthic invertebrates, and fish. The evaluation criteria developed as part of the evaluation can 

- 529 be used as guidance towards best practices to assess exposure, effects and effect threshold 

- 530 concentrations for MPs, and can provide a quantitative quality assessment of studies reporting 

- 531 adverse effects of MPs on aquatic organisms. Lastly, we summarize and discuss the 

- 532 characteristics of the tests that have been performed thus far (e.g., particle size ranges, 

- 533 concentrations, polymer types, particle shapes, species, endpoints, test duration) in order to 

- 534 detect knowledge gaps within effect studies, and use information gained from the review of the 

- 535 literature to assess the WOE with respect to the effect mechanisms most likely influenced by 536 exposure to MPs. 

- 537 When adopting strict quality criteria, an overall lack of reliability is observed in the studies 

- 538 evaluated in this review, particularly for how data from available effect studies can be used to 

- 539 help inform the risk assessment process. This is partly related to technical shortcomings in the 

- 540 experimental design, such as not ensuring chemical purity, prevention and verification of MP 

ACS Paragon Plus Environment 

Page 23 of 44 

Environmental Science & Technology 

- 541 contamination in the laboratory, and partly to limitations in the relevance of studies, for instance 

- 542 when studies do not use ecologically relevant particles or testing conditions. This implies that 

- 543 based on the current state-of-the-science, the WOE for ecological effects is very limited and the 

- 544 environmental risk of MPs is difficult to assess. The lack of clear evidence for ecological effects 

- 545 in nature due to relatively poor-quality effects studies available for the risk assessment process 

- 546 is worrying, particularly given concerns raised by the public and decision-makers to provide a 

- 547 quantitative assessment of the risks for MPs. The purpose of the present study is therefore to 548 provide timely guidance on best practices needed to improve and standardize effects testing 549 protocols. This includes the need for access to standardized test methods using reference MPs 

- 550 that can be used between research groups in an effort to strengthen both replication and inter551 and intra-laboratory reproducibility. We recommend that at least one of these reference 552 materials is an environmentally realistic mixture of particles, i.e. having a realistic range of 553 sizes, shapes, densities and ages. This way, organisms themselves select the fraction from the 554 mixture that is bioavailable and relevant for them. This would mimic the situation in nature 555 better than tests with single type materials. The adoption of standardized test methods and use 

- 556 of environmentally relevant reference materials would help reduce uncertainties inherent in the 557 effects data and strengthen both environmental risk assessment and mechanistic understanding 558 of the ecotoxicity of MP. 

- 559 Based on our review of study characteristics, it appears that particle type ‘fibers’ and polymer 

- 560 type ‘polypropylene’ are understudied in effect studies. Ideally, the MP tested should be as 

- 561 realistic as possible, thus representing a broad range of sizes, shapes, densities and polymer 

- 562 types. The ecological relevance of tests should be increased by extending exposure times, as 

- 563 chronic tests are rarely performed. In order for effect tests to be more informative for risk 

- 564 assessment, the reporting of thresholds effect concentrations should be made more accurate and 

ACS Paragon Plus Environment 

Page 24 of 44 

Environmental Science & Technology 

- 565 explicit, preferably as either LCx, ECx, LOEC or NOEC values, with the use of both mass and 

- 566 particle unit concentrations. 

- 567 Based on the evaluation of the WOE pertaining to effect mechanisms associated with exposure 

- 568 to MPs, we observe that the WOE is strongest for the mechanisms related to ‘inhibition of food 

- 569 assimilation and/or decreased nutritional value’, ‘internal physical damage’ and for the 

- 570 mechanism ‘external physical damage’. To increase the WOE of ecological effects and effect 

- 571 mechanisms we recommend that the guidance provided in this evaluation study be used to 

- 572 develop studies that explore the mechanistic nature of both MPs and generic particle effects on 

- 573 aquatic organisms more broadly.
