# **Microplastics sampling methods and standardization requirements in atmospheric environment—a critical review** 

**Bo Ren[1] · Yuchuan Meng[1] · Xiaofeng Yang[1] · Yu Chen[1] · Guodong Liu[1]** 

Received: 16 December 2024 / Accepted: 24 December 2025 / Published online: 9 February 2026 © The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer Nature B.V. 2026 

## **Abstract** 

The widespread use of plastics has greatly improved people's daily lives, but at the same time, it has also brought serious environmental challenges. Microplastics (MPs) pose a serious threat to ecosystems and human health due to their potential toxicity and ability to carry other pollutants. Especially MPs suspended in the atmosphere, which humans continuously inhale during daily breathing. The atmosphere, as an important link in the Earth's ecosystem, plays a crucial role in the global spread of MPs. Therefore, the research on atmospheric MPs has become a hot topic of global concern. Researchers used passive or active sampling methods to obtain atmospheric MPs samples, but there were significant differences in the sampling details among various studies. The inconsistency of sampling strategies and the non standardized sampling process limit the comparability and credibility of relevant research data. This review reviews the development history of atmospheric MPs and analyzes the sampling methods used to collect atmospheric MPs in 92 articles published between 2015 and 2023, including key factors such as sampling tools, time settings, sampling locations, sources, and intake flow rates. The focus was on comparing the advantages and disadvantages of different methods and evaluating the potential impact of the sampling process on the research results. To promote in-depth research on global atmospheric MPs pollution, it is urgent to establish standardized sampling methods to ensure the accuracy and comparability of data. Highlights Analyze and compare different sampling methods for atmospheric MPs. Establish a standardized sampling method for global atmospheric MPs. Non standardized sampling methods can affect the credibility and comparability of observation results. Summarize the current research status of global atmospheric MPs pollution. The atmosphere is the main transport pathway for MPs.

## **Introduction** 

The vast majority of people worldwide are exposed to unsafe air quality, largely due to pollutants emitted by human activities, including MPs (Rentschler & Leonova 2023). Due to the advantages of good stability, light weight, and easy processing of plastics, their application fields are becoming increasingly widespread (Shen et al. 2020). In the past 60 years, the global production of plastics has sharply increased. According to growth trends, it is estimated that by 2050, the demand for plastic will exceed 500 million tons, which is 1.6 times the production in 2014 and 33 times the consumption from 1960 to 1969 (Zheng et al. 2023). However, while plastics bring convenience to humanity, their environmental hazards are gradually becoming apparent. Approximately only 21% of the plastic waste generated globally is recycled or treated, while the remaining

Correspondingly, plastic pollution is becoming increasingly worrying. In 2004, (Thompson et al. 2004) first proposed the concept of "MPs". MPs are smaller in size, and their surface specificity leads to stronger adsorption of pollutants such as microorganisms and heavy metals, making them more easily ingested by organisms and absorbed by plants from their roots (Chen et al. 2020). The atmospheric environment is constantly in contact with humans, so people inadvertently inhale atmospheric MPs, which may pose a serious health threat (Fang et al. 2022). Especially in indoor air, people's intake of atmospheric MPs is believed to be much higher than through other exposure pathways (Geng et al. 2023). 

Currently, there is ongoing international debate regarding the definition of MPs and nanoplastics(NPs), though the most commonly accepted definition describes MPs as plastic particles with a diameter of less than 5 mm (Zhao et al. 2023). Then, with more attention paid to smaller plastic fragments, scientists proposed the concept of nanoplastics. NPs is a relatively new term that has emerged in recent years. The core of the debate is how to classify NPs(< 1000 nm or < 100 nm) (Bhat et al. 2023). At present, the recognized definition of plastic size classes used by most studies are macroplastics(> 5 mm), MPs (> 1 µm, < 5 mm) and nanoplastics(< 1 µm) (Azari et al. 2023). 

MPs are classified into two categories: primary MPs and secondary MPs (Cole et al. 2011). Primary MPs refer to small plastic particles produced directly for industrial purposes. These particles are commonly used as industrial raw materials and are found in everyday products such as cosmetics (e.g., exfoliating products), toothpaste, detergents, and certain polishing agents (Andrady 2017). These MPs particles may be discharged into aquatic environments via rivers, sewage treatment plants, and other pathways, eventually entering ecosystems. People have identified these products as a significant source of MPs in the environment, especially in the ocean (Rezania et al. 2018). Secondary MPs are formed from the breakdown of larger plastic waste through physical, chemical, and biological processes (Akdogan & Guven 2019). In areas with high population density, secondary MPs dominate, possibly due to frequent human activities (Anderson et al. 2017).The formation process leads to irregular shapes and wide size distributions of secondary MPs, which makes identification of secondary MPs difficult (Rezania et al. 2018). It is even speculated that further degradation of MPs may have adverse effects on the marine food web (Cole et al. 2011). 

In terms of MPs in the atmosphere, the shapes include film, fragment, fiber, granule and foam. But the main shape of MPs in the atmosphere is fibrous. (Szewc et al. 2021) believes that this may be related to the high production, large size, and easy separation of fibrous MPs from fabrics. Currently, research has indicated that shape is crucial for the migration process of atmospheric MPs. (Preston et al. 2023) stated that the atmosphere is an important carrier for the global transportation of MPs, and the shape has a greater impact on atmospheric migration than size. In addition to shape, the diverse chemical composition of atmospheric MPs is also an undeniable characteristic. There are hundreds of plastic materials used in daily life worldwide. The types of MPs in the atmosphere are influenced by the location of the study area, Because the sources of atmospheric MPs vary in different geographical locations. For example, the types of atmospheric MPs in Shanghai are mainly PA, PET, PP, and PVF (Jia et al. 2022). However, the types of atmospheric MPs in Mexico City are fiberglass, PE, PET, PA, and recycled cellulose (artificial silk) (Shruti et al. 2022). These polymers come from various items, such as PE and PP mainly used for plastic bags, PET for plastic bottles, PS for fast food boxes, and PA for engineering plastics (Aslam et al. 2023). After understanding the sources of atmospheric MPs, people can reduce their exposure to atmospheric MPs and implement policies that help reduce atmospheric MPs pollution in the future. Meanwhile, it is feasible to analyze the possible sources of marine atmospheric MPs based on polymer types (Ding et al. 2022). 

The threat of atmospheric MPs to ecosystems is receiving increasing attention, However, research on the environmental risks of atmospheric MPs is still in its early stages. Atmospheric MPs can enter water and soil under the influence of gravity, and can also affect other areas due to human or meteorological factors(Fig. 1). Many studies have shown that atmospheric MPs have the potential for long-distance transportation and exchange with MPs in other systems. (Hee et al. 2023) observed atmospheric MPs on pristine beaches and analyzed the long-distance atmospheric transport trajectories of MPs to indicate that monsoons affect the transportation of atmospheric MPs. Approximately 200 kg of atmospheric MPs are transported annually from Southeast Asia to Southeast China via atmospheric transport (Wang et al. 2021). (Liu et al. 2023a, b, c) observed a high degree of similarity between seawater and atmospheric MPs in terms of polymer type, shape, color, and size distribution, thus suggesting that atmospheric MPs are one of the sources of seawater MPs. The MPs entering the aquatic environment from the atmosphere can be ingested by various aquatic animals, aquatic microorganisms, and aquatic plants, which can easily have adverse effects on them and further lead to the deterioration of the ecosystem

This review collects and compares current research results on atmospheric MP to better understand the current status of atmospheric MP and reviews the sampling methods currently used to determine MP in ambient air and their impact on the results. Currently, there are two typical methods for sampling atmospheric MPs: passive sampling and active sampling. Passive sampling is simple but susceptible to external interference. Active sampling requires specialized personnel and equipment. Due to the lack of standardized sampling strategies, the various details of researchers are inconsistent, which has a significant impact on the comparison between atmospheric MPs studies. Although later researchers will refer to the sampling details of previous studies, there is currently no systematic sampling method for atmospheric MPs research worldwide. This review will provide a better understanding of the current sampling methods used and contribute to future research on the detection of MPs in the atmosphere. 

## **Materials and methods** 

The research data on atmospheric MPs worldwide was obtained through the use of the ISI Web of Science database (Fig. 2). The search strategy involves combining the term 'microplastic(s)' with keywords such as' air', 'atmosphere', 'airborne', and 'atmospheric'. The content of the article is filtered by these keywords in the title, abstract, and keyword sections. Then evaluate the relevance of these articles to the research objectives. The time range for retrieval is from 2015 to 2023, and research on atmospheric MPs began in 2015. 

Finally, Finally, all articles are further screened based on the following two criteria: (1) sampling MPs in the atmospheric environment at least once; (2) Detailed description of the sampling process. After review, 92 research articles were included in the scope of this review.

## **Research history of MPs in atmospheric environment** 

Research papers with an emphasis on MNPs in atmosphere are mostly published in the last eight years. For the first time, MPs have been recorded in atmospheric fallout in Paris (Dris et al. 2015). Cai and Wang conducted a preliminary study on the characteristics of MPs in atmospheric deposition in Dongguan City. This study found MPs in atmospheric deposition at all three sites, however, it was not determined whether MPs would be suspended in the air (Cai et al. 2017). On the streets of the central district of Tehran, The total of 2649 MPs particles was detected in 10 street dust samples. Among them, MPs were most frequent in 250 to 500 μm size class with a mean occurrence of 

33.7%, supporting the high probability of intake of MPs by inhabitants (Dehghani et al. 2017). Trainic and Flores determined airborne MPs particles are present in the open ocean, and investigated their possible sources (Trainic et al. 2020). Especially in the past two years, air quality has received more widespread attention, which is related to the health issues of residents. The research on atmospheric MPs has also developed rapidly as a result. In the early stages, due to the diverse range of sources, the varying characteristics of particles affecting their behavior and the range of environmental factors influencing particle transport, airborne MPs is extremely difficult to trace and predict (Horton & Dixon 2018). Research papers only analyzes the characteristics of MPs in the atmospheric environment, such as abundance, size, shape, and material. Recently, researchers have strengthened their research on the sources, transportation, health threats, and ecological risks of atmospheric MPs. (Perera et al. 2023) observed indoor atmospheric MPs in different environments and evaluated the exposure levels of indoor people to atmospheric MPs. The vertical distribution of atmospheric MPs is driven by unstable atmospheric conditions, which may lead to local accumulation of atmospheric MPs (Yuan et al. 2023a, b). Although studies related to MPs started in 2004, Compared to the plethora of MPs studies in aquatic environment and growing number of studies in terrestrial environments, research on atmospheric MPs has only recently gained attention (Zhang et al. 2020a, b). However, there is still a significant gap between research on atmospheric MPs and what remains to be discovered (Yuan et al. 2022).

## **Sampling methods for atmospheric MPs** 

The current situation of air pollution in various parts of the world is very serious, and the concentration of total suspended particulate matter in urban atmospheric environment generally exceeds the standard. As a type of total suspended particulate matter, MPs can be collected through traditional sampling methods, including passive sampling and active sampling (Lai et al. 2021). At different stages of atmospheric MPs research, the choice of sampling techniques largely depends on the research question and location. The purpose of sampling is to collect MPs in the atmosphere while avoiding the collection of other suspended particles such as dust and organic matter. Unfortunately, the current target of all sampling strategies is the total suspended particulate matter in the atmosphere. In this review, 54 papers only used passive sampling methods, 40 papers only used active sampling methods, and 7 papers used both passive and active sampling methods simultaneously. ## **Passive sampling methods** 

For research, passive sampling can be used to continuously collect MPs depositions in the atmosphere and store them in containers. Traditionally, there are two methods for passive sampling, namely collecting ground dust and collecting dry and wet depositions. Although a common goal is to investigate MPs in the atmospheric environment, the sampling instruments used in different studies vary. 

To ensure obtaining the most representative dust samples, careful consideration should be given to factors such as sampling time and location, sample quantity, and experimental objectives. Further consideration should be given to whether human activities at the sampling site will have an impact on the abundance of captured MPs. The method of collecting dust, while simple to operate, time-efficient, and suitable for various areas, also has its limitations. On the one hand, due to the uncertainty of ground dust sources, researchers are unable to determine whether microplastics originate from atmospheric deposition and cannot reflect the current status of atmospheric microplastic pollution. On the other hand, there are many impurities in dust that are difficult to handle. Wood and paper exhibit behavior similar to MPs under fluorescence and polarized light microscopy (Abbasi et al. 2017). This explains why only 7 papers have used the dust collection method.

## **Active sampling methods** 

Active sampling is one of the traditional sampling methods for atmospheric pollutants, which involves collecting MPs particles from the atmosphere onto a filter membrane using a air sampler. The air sampler generally consists of an operating platform, a pump, a filtering device and an intake pipe. The sampler is connected to a power source and uses a pump to actively pump air into the filtering device. The air passes through different aperture filtering devices, ultimately leaving MPs on the filter membrane. The application of atmospheric samplers is very extensive, mainly used for studying the sources and pollution mechanisms of atmospheric substances, in order to evaluate atmospheric quality and control atmospheric pollution. The research selected for this review was conducted in urban areas, coastal regions, and oceans. The active sampling method has been used for ocean atmospheric sampling in 12 studies.(Table 3).

## **Comparison between active sampling and passive samping** 

Passive sampling technology is easy to operate, does not require power, and is easy to maintain, making it suitable for various sampling conditions, and can achieve synchronous monitoring of atmospheric MPs at multiple locations over a large area. Active sampling is convenient and consumes less time. The variables such as air flow rate, sampling volume, and filter aperture are controllable and highly reproducible. The method of collecting dust has not been used in recent years. The MPs in dust are not entirely derived from atmospheric deposition, so the collection results of this method are not representative. This can serve as a supplement to the detection results of atmospheric MPs deposition method. 

According to the global distribution of sampling points (Fig. 6), its distribution emphasizes the global nature of active sampling, while the spatial distribution characteristics of passive sampling are more pronounced. Active sampling has become the main technique for capturing MPs in the ocean atmosphere. Passive sampling is greatly influenced by natural factors such as wind speed, climate, ocean flow, etc., which poses many challenges in capturing MPs in the ocean atmosphere. For example, high wind speeds or rainfall can affect the collection of deposition, leading to inaccurate results (Sun et al. 2022). In addition, when working on the ocean, tools also move with the ship, which greatly affects the collection efficiency of passive sampling devices. Due to the interference of these natural factors, passive sampling methods often cannot stably and accurately capture MPs in marine air, so researchers generally choose active sampling methods. ## **Data comparability** 

MP is a pollutant in the air that people breathe, but current research mainly analyzes the quantity, shape, color, chemical composition, and size of atmospheric MPs. The sampling process should make the original sample as representative as possible and reduce sampling errors. All equipment used during the sampling process should avoid contamination of the sample, or the equipment with the least impact on the sample should be selected. ## **Precautions for effectively capturing MPs from the atmosphere** 

The existing sampling methods have a clear risk of sample contamination during the process from collection to analysis. Despite numerous efforts made by researchers to reduce risks. For example, placing the sampling equipment on the rooftop or away from the ground, cleaning the equipment with water before and after sampling, and recording meteorological data during sampling (Edo et al. 2023; Huang et al. 2021; Purwiyanto et al. 2022; Sun et al. 2022). Accurately describing the current status of MPs in the atmosphere requires collecting sufficient amounts of MPs from the air, which is largely influenced by sampling methods that still lack standardization in this field. Developing standardized 

sampling methods for the study of atmospheric MPs is also crucial for in-depth research on the various characteristics of MPs in the atmosphere, and is an urgent problem that needs to be addressed in current research. 

In current research, researchers should provide clear and detailed information on sampling equipment, methods, sampling locations, selection criteria, sampling processes, etc., in order to provide as much information as possible for robust basic research, improve comparability between studies, and accelerate future researchers' innovation in sampling methods. Sampling will have a significant impact on the characteristics of MPs suspended in the air and ultimately affect the research significance of their hazards. In this case, it is necessary to utilize existing technologies to make executable and research purpose oriented sampling method selections. 

## **Recommendations and future developments** 

This review summarizes the existing research, latest developments, current status of sampling methods, and their importance to the results of MPs in the atmosphere, and points out the necessity of sampling standardization. Previous studies have detected MPs in atmospheric samples collected from cities, suburbs, oceans, and even polar regions. But as mentioned earlier, these studies only revealed the presence of MPs in the atmosphere, including size, abundance, deposition rate, chemical composition, and color. Further research is needed to better understand the risk levels associated with these MPs. At present, research on atmospheric MPs mainly focuses on the emission, transportation, and migration of MPs into the air, as well as ecological risk and Health threats assessment. However, in existing reports, the understanding of MPs in the air is still very limited, and more extensive monitoring research is needed to better understand the fate of MPs in ambient air. The field of ecological toxicology research on atmospheric MPs is almost unexplored. Although the health risks of MPs are a consensus among most researchers, laboratory evidence must be obtained to assess their impact on ecosystems and humans. Researchers have conducted field experiments or mathematical models to assess the amount of atmospheric MPs inhaled by people (Catarino et al. 2018; Chen et al. 2024; Fang et al. 2022; Vianello et al. 2019). Future research can focus more on the sources of MPs and their fate after being inhaled by people.

In terms of sample collection, based on the research experience of air particulate matter, such as PM10, PM2.5, and air quality monitoring, this study proposes the following suggestions. 

- 1) Provide a detailed explanation of the reasons for selecting the location and number of sampling points. At the beginning, necessary on-site observation should be carried out at the sampling site, without any obstacles that may hinder air flow or other factors that may interfere with the sampling. 

- 2) To prevent dust from other sources from entering the sample, the height of the sampling inlet should be set at 1–1.5 m above the ground. 

- 3) The opening area of the container used to collect dry and wet sediments should be consistent and can be adjusted using a funnel. 

- 4) The duration of each sampling event should be uniformly specified, preferably 24 h, and an interval of 3–5 days must be set between two sampling events at the same sampling point. 

- 5) The sampling equipment should be made of non plastic materials and have no adsorption effect on MPs. Glass and stainless steel are excellent choices. 

- 6) At the beginning of sampling, meteorological parameters and weather conditions should be observed and recorded. The recorded content must be standardized and have a unified format. 

- 7) The filter membrane used with active sampling equipment should be made of non plastic materials such as glass fiber filter membrane and quartz filter membrane. The key is that the pore size should be consistent, and the currently widely used 1.6 μm can be chosen. 

- 8) The intake flow rate of the active sampler should be kept constant throughout the entire process. The intake flow rate can be selected according to the environment of the sampling location. For example, based on previous researchers' intake flow settings and differences in the abundance of atmospheric MPs in different environments, We can choose 16.67L/min for indoor sampling, 100L/ min for outdoor sampling and 1 m[3] /min for Ocean air.
